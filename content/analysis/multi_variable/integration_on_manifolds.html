Diffeomorphism
If \( U,V \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) are open, a diffeomorphism \( h: U \to V \) is a bijection such that both \( h \) and its inverse function \( h ^ { -1 } \) are \( C ^ \infty \).
\( k \)-Dimensional Manifold in Euclidean Space
A subset \( M \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) is a \( k \)-dimensional manifold if for every \( x \in M \), there are open sets \( U,V \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) with \( x \in U \), and a diffeomorphism \( h: U \to V \), such that \[ h\left( U \cap M \right) = V \cap \left( \mathbb{ R } ^ k \times \left\{ 0 \right\} \right). \]
Regular Level Set is a Manifold
Let \( A \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) be open and let \( g: A \to \mathbb{ R } ^ p \) be \( C ^ \infty \). If the Jacobian matrix \( g'\left( x \right) \) has rank \( p \) whenever \( g\left( x \right)=0 \), then \[ g ^ { -1 }\left( 0 \right) \] is an \( \left( n-p \right) \)-dimensional manifold in \( \mathbb{ R } ^ n \).
Let \( x\in g ^ { -1 }\left( 0 \right) \), and write the coordinate variables in \( \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) as \( z_1,\ldots,z_n \). The Jacobian matrix \( g'\left( x \right) \) is a \( p\times n \) matrix whose \( j \)-th column is the derivative of \( g \) in the \( z_j \)-direction.

Since \( g'\left( x \right) \) has rank \( p \), its \( n \) columns span a \( p \)-dimensional subspace of \( \mathbb{ R } ^ p \). Therefore some \( p \) of those \( n \) columns form a basis of \( \mathbb{ R } ^ p \). Choose their indices and call them \( j_1,\ldots,j_p \).

Let \( v=\left( z_{j_1},\ldots,z_{j_p} \right) \), and let \( u \) be the remaining \( n-p \) coordinate variables. With the coordinates ordered as \( \left( u,v \right)\in\mathbb{ R } ^ { n-p }\times\mathbb{ R } ^ p \), the \( p\times p \) matrix \[ \left[ \frac{\partial g ^ i}{\partial v_j}\left( x \right) \right]_{1\le i,j\le p} \] has those selected columns as its columns. Since those columns form a basis of \( \mathbb{ R } ^ p \), this matrix is invertible.

By the implicit function theorem, after restricting to a small neighborhood, the equation \( g\left( u,v \right)=0 \) uniquely determines \( v \) as a \( C ^ \infty \) function \( v=\varphi\left( u \right) \). Define \[ h\left( u,v \right)=\left( u,v-\varphi\left( u \right) \right). \] Then \( h \) is a local diffeomorphism, and it sends the local set \( g ^ { -1 }\left( 0 \right) \) exactly to \( \mathbb{ R } ^ { n-p }\times\left\{0\right\} \). This is the local model required in the definition of a manifold, so \( g ^ { -1 }\left( 0 \right) \) is an \( \left( n-p \right) \)-dimensional manifold.

Unit Sphere is a 2-Dimensional Manifold
The unit sphere \[ S ^ 2 = \left\{ \left( x,y,z \right)\in \mathbb{ R } ^ 3 : x ^ 2+y ^ 2+z ^ 2=1 \right\} \] is a 2-dimensional manifold in Euclidean space.
Define \( g: \mathbb{ R } ^ 3 \to \mathbb{ R } \) by \[ g\left( x,y,z \right) = x ^ 2+y ^ 2+z ^ 2-1. \] Then \( S ^ 2 = g ^ { -1 }\left( 0 \right) \), and \( g \) is \( C ^ \infty \). Its Jacobian matrix is \[ g'\left( x,y,z \right) = \begin{bmatrix} 2x & 2y & 2z \end{bmatrix}. \] If \( \left( x,y,z \right)\in S ^ 2 \), then \( x ^ 2+y ^ 2+z ^ 2=1 \), so not all of \( x,y,z \) are zero. Therefore \( g'\left( x,y,z \right) \) has rank \(1\) on \( g ^ { -1 }\left( 0 \right) \). By the regular level set theorem, \( S ^ 2 \) is a \( \left( 3-1 \right) \)-dimensional manifold in \( \mathbb{ R } ^ 3 \), so it is a 2-dimensional manifold.
Coordinate System on a Manifold
Let \( M \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) be a \( k \)-dimensional manifold. A coordinate system around \( x \in M \) is the function \( f:W\to\mathbb{ R } ^ n \), where \( W\subseteq\mathbb{ R } ^ k \) is the coordinate domain and \( f\left( W \right) \) is the patch of \( M \) being described. The function \( f \) must satisfy:
  • \( W \) is open in \( \mathbb{ R } ^ k \), and there is an open set \( U\subseteq\mathbb{ R } ^ n \) such that \( f\left( W \right)=M\cap U \) and \( x\in f\left( W \right) \).
  • \( f \) is one-to-one and \( C ^ \infty \).
  • The Jacobian matrix \( f'\left( y \right) \) has rank \( k \) for every \( y\in W \).
  • The inverse \( f ^ { -1 }:f\left( W \right)\to W \) is continuous.
A point \( y\in W \) is a coordinate value, and \( f\left( y \right)\in M \) is the corresponding point on the manifold.
Upper Hemisphere Coordinate System on the Sphere
Let \[ S ^ 2 = \left\{ \left( x,y,z \right)\in\mathbb{ R } ^ 3 : x ^ 2+y ^ 2+z ^ 2=1 \right\} \] and let \[ W=\left\{ \left( u,v \right)\in\mathbb{ R } ^ 2 : u ^ 2+v ^ 2 \lt 1 \right\}. \] Show that \( f:W\to\mathbb{ R } ^ 3 \), defined by \[ f\left( u,v \right) = \left( u,v,\sqrt{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2} \right), \] is a coordinate system around every point of the upper hemisphere \( S ^ 2\cap\left\{ z\gt 0 \right\} \).
The set \( W \) is open in \( \mathbb{ R } ^ 2 \). The map \( f \) is \( C ^ \infty \) on \( W \), because \( 1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2 \gt 0 \) there and the square root function is smooth on positive real numbers.

The map \( f \) is one-to-one: if \( f\left( u_1,v_1 \right)=f\left( u_2,v_2 \right) \), then comparing the first two coordinates gives \( u_1=u_2 \) and \( v_1=v_2 \).

Let \( U=\left\{ \left( x,y,z \right)\in\mathbb{ R } ^ 3 : z\gt 0 \right\} \). Then \[ f\left( W \right)=S ^ 2\cap U. \] Indeed, every \( f\left( u,v \right) \) lies on \( S ^ 2 \) and has positive \( z \)-coordinate. Conversely, if \( \left( x,y,z \right)\in S ^ 2\cap U \), then \( x ^ 2+y ^ 2=1-z ^ 2\lt 1 \), so \( \left( x,y \right)\in W \), and because \( z\gt 0 \), \[ z=\sqrt{1-x ^ 2-y ^ 2}. \] Thus \( \left( x,y,z \right)=f\left( x,y \right) \).

The inverse function on the image is \[ f ^ { -1 }:S ^ 2\cap U\to W, \qquad f ^ { -1 }\left( x,y,z \right)=\left( x,y \right), \] which is continuous.

Finally, the Jacobian matrix is \[ f'\left( u,v \right) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ \frac{-u}{\sqrt{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2}} & \frac{-v}{\sqrt{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2}} \end{bmatrix}. \] Its first two rows form the \( 2\times 2 \) identity matrix, so its columns are linearly independent and the matrix has rank \(2\). Therefore \( f \) satisfies every condition in the definition of a coordinate system around every point of \( S ^ 2\cap\left\{ z\gt 0 \right\} \).

Standard Coordinate System on the \( x \)-Axis
Let \[ M=\mathbb{ R }\times\left\{0\right\}\subseteq\mathbb{ R } ^ 2. \] Show that \( f:\mathbb{ R }\to\mathbb{ R } ^ 2 \), defined by \[ f\left( t \right)=\left( t,0 \right), \] is a coordinate system around every point of \( M \).
Let \( x=\left( a,0 \right)\in M \). The domain \( W=\mathbb{ R } \) is open in \( \mathbb{ R } \), and \( f \) is one-to-one, because \( f\left( s \right)=f\left( t \right) \) implies \( \left( s,0 \right)=\left( t,0 \right) \), so \( s=t \).

The map \( f \) is \( C ^ \infty \), and its Jacobian matrix is \[ f'\left( t \right) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}. \] This matrix has rank \( 1 \) for every \( t\in\mathbb{ R } \).

Taking \( U=\mathbb{ R } ^ 2 \), we have \( f\left( W \right)=M=M\cap U \). The inverse function on the image is \[ f ^ { -1 } : M\to\mathbb{ R }, \qquad f ^ { -1 }\left( t,0 \right)=t, \] which is continuous. Therefore \( f \) satisfies every condition in the definition of a coordinate system around \( x \). Since \( x\in M \) was arbitrary, \( f \) is a coordinate system around every point of \( M \).

Induced Volume of a Manifold Subset
Let \(M\subseteq\mathbb R^n\) be a \(k\)-dimensional manifold, and let \(E\subseteq M\). Suppose first that \(E\subseteq f\left(W\right)\) for a coordinate system \(f:W\to\mathbb R^n\). The induced \(k\)-dimensional volume of \(E\) is \[ \operatorname{vol}_M\left(E\right) = \int_{f^{-1}\left(E\right)} \sqrt{ \det \left( f'\left(u\right)^T f'\left(u\right) \right) }\;du, \] whenever this integral exists. The matrix \(f'\left(u\right)^T f'\left(u\right)\) records the inner products of the coordinate tangent vectors, so its determinant gives the squared \(k\)-dimensional volume distortion of the coordinate map at \(u\).

If \(E\) is not contained in one coordinate image, decompose \(E\) into countably many disjoint measurable pieces, each contained in a coordinate image, and add the above values. The change-of-variables formula makes the result independent of the chosen coordinate systems. When \(k=2\), this induced volume is called the surface area of \(E\), and is denoted \(\operatorname{area}_M\left(E\right)\).

Surface Area Measure
Let \(M\subseteq\mathbb R^n\) be a two-dimensional manifold. The surface area measure on \(M\), denoted \(A_M\), is the measure whose value on a measurable subset \(E\subseteq M\) is the surface area of \(E\): \[ A_M(E)=\operatorname{area}_M(E). \] Thus an expression such as \[ \int_M f(q)\,dA_M(q) \] means integration with respect to the surface area measure \(A_M\). The symbol \(A_M\) denotes a measure on subsets of \(M\), not a scalar-valued function of the point \(q\).
Surface Area Density in Coordinates
Let \(M\subseteq\mathbb R^3\) be a two-dimensional manifold, and let \[ f:U\to M, \qquad U\subseteq\mathbb R^2, \] be a coordinate system. The surface area density of \(M\) in the coordinates \(f\) is the function \[ \rho_f:U\to\mathbb R^{\ge0} \] defined by \[ \rho_f(u) = \sqrt{ \det\left( f'\left(u\right)^T f'\left(u\right) \right) }. \] This is the factor that appears in the induced volume definition when \(k=2\). Thus locally, \[ dA=\rho_f(u)\,du. \]
Surface Area Density Cross Product Formula
Let \(M\subseteq\mathbb R^3\) be a two-dimensional manifold, and let \(f:U\to M\) be a coordinate system with \(U\subseteq\mathbb R^2\). Define the coordinate tangent vectors \[ f_1(u)=\frac{\partial f}{\partial u_1}(u), \qquad f_2(u)=\frac{\partial f}{\partial u_2}(u). \] Then the surface area density in the coordinates \(f\) is \[ \rho_f(u) = \left\|f_1(u)\times f_2(u)\right\|. \]
The Jacobian matrix \(f'\left(u\right)\) has columns \(f_1(u)\) and \(f_2(u)\). Therefore \[ f'\left(u\right)^T f'\left(u\right) = \begin{bmatrix} f_1(u)\cdot f_1(u) & f_1(u)\cdot f_2(u)\\ f_2(u)\cdot f_1(u) & f_2(u)\cdot f_2(u) \end{bmatrix}. \] Its determinant is \[ \left(f_1\cdot f_1\right)\left(f_2\cdot f_2\right) - \left(f_1\cdot f_2\right)^2, \] where the argument \(u\) is suppressed to reduce notation. By the component formula for the cross product and the definition of dot product, \[ \left\|f_1\times f_2\right\|^2 = \left(f_1\cdot f_1\right)\left(f_2\cdot f_2\right) - \left(f_1\cdot f_2\right)^2. \] Hence \[ \det\left(f'\left(u\right)^T f'\left(u\right)\right) = \left\|f_1(u)\times f_2(u)\right\|^2. \] Taking the nonnegative square root in the definition of surface area density gives \[ \rho_f(u) = \left\|f_1(u)\times f_2(u)\right\|. \]
Induced Volume of the Unit Square
Let \[ M=\mathbb{ R } ^ 2\times\left\{0\right\}\subseteq\mathbb{ R } ^ 3 \] and let \[ E=\left[0,1\right]\times\left[0,1\right]\times\left\{0\right\}\subseteq M. \] Use the induced volume of a manifold subset to compute the surface area of \(E\).
Use the coordinate system \(f:\mathbb{ R } ^ 2\to\mathbb{ R } ^ 3\) defined by \[ f\left( u,v \right)=\left( u,v,0 \right). \] Then \(f^{-1}\left(E\right)=\left[0,1\right]\times\left[0,1\right]\), and the Jacobian matrix is \[ f'\left( u,v \right) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1\\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}. \] Therefore \[ f'\left( u,v \right)^T f'\left( u,v \right) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \] so its determinant is \(1\). By the definition of induced volume, \[ \operatorname{area}_M\left(E\right) = \int_{\left[0,1\right]\times\left[0,1\right]} \sqrt{1}\;du\,dv = 1. \] Thus the induced surface area of the unit square is \(1\), as expected.
Induced Area of the Projected Unit Disk on the Sphere
Let \[ S ^ 2 = \left\{ \left( x,y,z \right)\in\mathbb{ R } ^ 3 : x ^ 2+y ^ 2+z ^ 2=1 \right\}. \] Project the open unit disk \[ W=\left\{ \left( u,v \right)\in\mathbb{ R } ^ 2 : u ^ 2+v ^ 2\lt1 \right\} \] onto the top of the unit sphere by \[ f\left( u,v \right)=\left( u,v,\sqrt{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2} \right). \] Use the induced volume of a manifold subset to compute the surface area of \( f\left( W \right) \).
The Jacobian matrix of \( f \) is \[ f'\left( u,v \right) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1\\ -\frac{u}{\sqrt{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2}} & -\frac{v}{\sqrt{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2}} \end{bmatrix}. \] Hence \[ f'\left( u,v \right)^T f'\left( u,v \right) = \begin{bmatrix} 1+\frac{u ^ 2}{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2} & \frac{uv}{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2}\\ \frac{uv}{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2} & 1+\frac{v ^ 2}{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2} \end{bmatrix}. \] Its determinant is \[ \left( 1+\frac{u ^ 2}{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2} \right) \left( 1+\frac{v ^ 2}{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2} \right) - \left( \frac{uv}{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2} \right) ^ 2 = \frac{1}{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2}. \] Therefore \[ \operatorname{area}_{S ^ 2}\left( f\left( W \right) \right) = \int_W \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2}}\;du\,dv. \] The integrand becomes unbounded as \(u ^ 2+v ^ 2\to1\), so we first compute the area on smaller disks that stay away from the boundary circle. These smaller disks exhaust \(W\), and the final area is obtained by taking their limit using monotone convergence.

For \(0\lt\rho\lt1\), let \[ W_\rho=\left\{ \left( u,v \right)\in\mathbb{ R } ^ 2:u ^ 2+v ^ 2\lt \rho ^ 2 \right\}. \]

Apply the polar coordinate change of variables on \(W_\rho\) with \[ q\left( u,v \right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-u ^ 2-v ^ 2}}. \] Since \[ q\left( r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta \right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-r ^ 2}}, \] the corollary gives \[ \operatorname{area}_{S ^ 2}\left( f\left( W_\rho \right) \right) = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\rho \frac{r}{\sqrt{1-r ^ 2}}\;dr\,d\theta \] To evaluate the inner integral, use the substitution \(s=1-r ^ 2\), so \(ds=-2r\,dr\). Therefore \[ \int_0^\rho \frac{r}{\sqrt{1-r ^ 2}}\;dr = -\frac{1}{2} \int_1^{1-\rho ^ 2} s ^ { -1/2 }\;ds = \left[ -\sqrt{1-r ^ 2} \right] _ { 0 } ^ { \rho } = 1-\sqrt{1-\rho ^ 2}, \] where the bracket evaluation uses the fundamental theorem of calculus. Hence \[ \operatorname{area}_{S ^ 2}\left( f\left( W_\rho \right) \right) = \int_0^{2\pi} \left( 1-\sqrt{1-\rho ^ 2} \right)\;d\theta = 2\pi\left( 1-\sqrt{1-\rho ^ 2} \right). \] As \( \rho\to1^- \), the sets \( W_\rho \) increase to \( W \). Since \( q\ge0 \), the functions \( q\,\mathbf{1}_{W_\rho} \) increase pointwise to \( q\,\mathbf{1}_W \). By the monotone convergence theorem, \[ \int_W q\left( u,v \right)\;du\,dv = \lim_{\rho\to1^-} \int_{W_\rho} q\left( u,v \right)\;du\,dv. \] Therefore taking the limit as \( \rho\to1^- \) gives \[ \operatorname{area}_{S ^ 2}\left( f\left( W \right) \right) = 2\pi. \] Thus the projected unit disk covers the upper hemisphere, whose induced surface area is \(2\pi\).
Coordinate Characterization of Manifolds
A subset \( M \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) is a \( k \)-dimensional manifold if and only if every point of \( M \) has a coordinate system around it.
Suppose first that \( M \) is a \( k \)-dimensional manifold. Choose a diffeomorphism \( h:U\to V \) giving the local manifold model. Let \[ W=\left\{ a\in\mathbb{ R } ^ k:\left( a,0 \right)\in h\left( M\cap U \right) \right\} \] and define \( f:W\to\mathbb{ R } ^ n \) by \( f\left( a \right)=h ^ { -1 }\left( a,0 \right) \). This gives \( f\left( W \right)=M\cap U \), and the inverse coordinate map is continuous. If \( H \) denotes the first \( k \) components of \( h \), then \( H\circ f \) is the identity on \( W \), so \( H'\left( f\left( y \right) \right)f'\left( y \right)=I \). Hence \( f'\left( y \right) \) has rank \( k \).

Conversely, suppose \( f:W\to\mathbb{ R } ^ n \) satisfies the coordinate condition near \( x=f\left( y \right) \). Choose \( k \) component functions of \( f \) whose \( k\times k \) derivative matrix has nonzero determinant. After reordering coordinates, define \[ G\left( a,b \right)=f\left( a \right)+\left( 0,b \right), \qquad b\in\mathbb{ R } ^ { n-k }. \] Then \( G'\left( y,0 \right) \) is invertible. By the inverse function theorem, \( G \) has a local differentiable inverse \( h \). Since \( f ^ { -1 } \) is continuous on \( f\left( W \right) \), shrinking the neighborhoods gives \[ h\left( V\cap M \right)=V'\cap\left( \mathbb{ R } ^ k\times\left\{0\right\} \right). \] This is exactly the local model for a \( k \)-dimensional manifold.

Half-Space
The half-space \( H ^ k \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ k \) is \[ H ^ k = \left\{ x \in \mathbb{ R } ^ k: x ^ k \ge 0 \right\}. \]
Manifold with Boundary
A subset \( M \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) is a \( k \)-dimensional manifold with boundary if every point of \( M \) has a local diffeomorphic model either on \( \mathbb{ R } ^ k \times \left\{0\right\} \) or on \( H ^ k \) \( \times \left\{0\right\} \). Points modeled on the boundary \( \left\{ x ^ k=0 \right\} \) form the boundary of the manifold, denoted \( \partial M \).
Boundary of a Manifold with Boundary is a Manifold
If \( M \) is a \( k \)-dimensional manifold with boundary, then \( \partial M \) is a \( \left( k-1 \right) \)-dimensional manifold and \( M\setminus\partial M \) is a \( k \)-dimensional manifold.
Let \( x\in M \). By the definition of manifold with boundary, a neighborhood of \( x \) is locally diffeomorphic either to \( \mathbb{ R } ^ k\times\left\{0\right\} \) or to \( H ^ k\times\left\{0\right\} \).

If \( x\notin\partial M \), then in local coordinates \( M \) is modeled on the interior of \( H ^ k \), which is locally \( \mathbb{ R } ^ k \). Thus \( M\setminus\partial M \) is locally a \( k \)-dimensional manifold.

If \( x\in\partial M \), then a boundary chart carries \( \partial M \) locally to \[ \left\{ y\in H ^ k : y ^ k=0 \right\}\times\left\{0\right\}, \] which is diffeomorphic to \( \mathbb{ R } ^ { k-1 }\times\left\{0\right\} \). Therefore \( \partial M \) is a \( \left( k-1 \right) \)-dimensional manifold.

Tangent Space of a Manifold
Let \( M \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) be a \( k \)-dimensional manifold and let \( x=f\left( a \right) \) for a coordinate system \( f: W \to \mathbb{ R } ^ n \). The tangent space of \( M \) at \( x \) is \[ M _ x = Df\left( a \right)\left( \mathbb{ R } ^ k \right). \] Here \(Df\left( a \right)\) is the derivative linear transformation of \(f\) at \(a\), and \(Df\left( a \right)\left( \mathbb{ R } ^ k \right)\) means the image of the set \(\mathbb{ R } ^ k\) under that linear transformation: \[ Df\left( a \right)\left( \mathbb{ R } ^ k \right) = \left\{ Df\left( a \right)\left( v \right): v\in\mathbb{ R } ^ k \right\}. \] This subspace is independent of the coordinate system.
Vector Field on a Manifold
A vector field on a manifold \( M \) assigns to each \( x \in M \) a vector \( F\left( x \right)\in \)\( M _ x \). It is differentiable if its coordinate representation is differentiable in every coordinate system.
Differential Form on a Manifold
A \( p \)-form on a manifold \( M \) assigns to each \( x \in M \) an alternating tensor in \( \Lambda ^ p\left( M _ x \right) \), where \( M_x \) is the tangent space of \( M \) at \( x \). It is differentiable if its pullback by every coordinate system is differentiable.
Exterior Derivative on Manifolds
There is a unique \( \left( p+1 \right) \)-form \( d\omega \) on \( M \) such that for every coordinate system \( f: W \to \mathbb{ R } ^ n \), \[ f ^ *\left( d\omega \right) = d\left( f ^ *\omega \right). \]
Let \( f:W\to\mathbb{ R } ^ n \) be a coordinate system with \( x=f\left( a \right) \), and let \( v_1,\ldots,v_{p+1}\in M_x \). Since \( f'\left( a \right) \) identifies \( \mathbb{ R } ^ k \) with the tangent space \( M_x \), there are unique \( w_1,\ldots,w_{p+1}\in\mathbb{ R } ^ k \) such that \( f'\left( a \right)w_i=v_i \).

Define \[ d\omega\left( x \right)\left( v_1,\ldots,v_{p+1} \right) = d\left( f ^ *\omega \right)\left( a \right)\left( w_1,\ldots,w_{p+1} \right). \] The right side uses the exterior derivative on the coordinate domain. The compatibility of coordinate changes shows that this value does not depend on the coordinate system \( f \), so \( d\omega \) is well-defined.

This construction gives \( f ^ *\left( d\omega \right)=d\left( f ^ *\omega \right) \) in every coordinate system. It is also forced by that equation, so the form \( d\omega \) is unique.

Orientable Manifold
A manifold \( M \) is orientable if its tangent spaces \( M _ x \) can be assigned orientations consistently across coordinate systems. A manifold together with such a choice is an oriented manifold.
Induced Boundary Orientation
If \( M \) is an oriented manifold with boundary, the induced orientation on \( \partial M \) is determined by requiring that \[ \left[ n\left( x \right),v _ 1,\ldots,v _ { k-1 } \right] \] have the orientation of \( M _ x \), where \( n\left( x \right) \) is the outward unit normal and \( v _ 1,\ldots,v _ { k-1 } \in \left( \partial M \right) _ x \).
Integral of a Form over a Manifold
If \( M \) is an oriented \( k \)-dimensional manifold and \( \omega \) is a \( k \)-form on \( M \), the integral \( \int _ M \omega \) is defined by pulling \( \omega \) back to orientation-preserving coordinate systems and assembling the local integrals with a partition of unity.
Stokes' Theorem on Manifolds
If \( M \) is a compact oriented \( k \)-dimensional manifold with boundary and \( \omega \) is a \( \left( k-1 \right) \)-form on \( M \), then \[ \int _ M d\omega = \int _ { \partial M } \omega, \] where \( \partial M \) has the induced orientation.
First suppose \( \omega \) is supported in the image of a single orientation-preserving singular \( k \)-cube \( c \) lying in \( M\setminus\partial M \). By the definition of exterior derivative on a manifold and the cube version of Stokes' theorem, \[ \int_M d\omega = \int_c d\omega = \int_{\partial c}\omega. \] Since \( \omega \) is zero on \( \partial c \) and also zero on \( \partial M \), both sides of the theorem are zero.

Next suppose \( \omega \) is supported in one singular \( k \)-cube \( c \) whose only face in \( \partial M \) is \( c_{(k,0)} \). The induced boundary orientation was chosen so that the sign of this face agrees with the boundary integral. Therefore the cube Stokes theorem gives \[ \int_M d\omega = \int_{\partial M}\omega. \]

In the general case, choose an open cover and a partition of unity \( \Phi \) so that each \( \varphi\omega \), for \( \varphi\in\Phi \), has one of the two supported forms above. Since \( M \) is compact, only finitely many terms contribute. Also, \[ \sum_{\varphi\in\Phi} d\varphi\wedge\omega=0 \] because \( \sum_{\varphi\in\Phi}\varphi=1 \). Hence \[ \int_M d\omega = \sum_{\varphi\in\Phi}\int_M d\left( \varphi\omega \right) = \sum_{\varphi\in\Phi}\int_{\partial M}\varphi\omega = \int_{\partial M}\omega. \]

Volume Element on a Manifold
If \( M \) is an oriented \( k \)-dimensional manifold with the inner product inherited from \( \mathbb{ R } ^ n \), the volume element \( dV \) is the \( k \)-form assigning to each \( x \in M \) the volume element of the oriented inner product space \( M _ x \). The volume of \( M \) is \[ \int _ M dV \] whenever this integral exists.
Surface Area Element in \( \mathbb{ R } ^ 3 \)
If \( M \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ 3 \) is an oriented surface with unit normal \( n \), then its area element \( dA \) satisfies \[ dA\left( v,w \right) = \left( v \times w,n \right) \] for positively oriented tangent vectors \( v,w \in M _ x \).
At \( x\in M \), the volume element on the two-dimensional tangent space \( M_x \) is the oriented area form. If \( v,w\in M_x \) are positively oriented tangent vectors, then \( v\times w \) is parallel to the chosen unit normal \( n \).

The scalar \( \left( v\times w,n \right) \) is the signed area of the parallelogram spanned by \( v \) and \( w \), positive exactly when \( v,w \) have the orientation determined by \( n \). Therefore the oriented area element satisfies \[ dA\left( v,w \right)=\left( v\times w,n \right). \]

Green's Theorem
Let \( M \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ 2 \) be a compact oriented two-dimensional manifold with boundary, with the usual orientation. If \( \alpha,\beta: M \to \mathbb{ R } \) are differentiable, then \[ \int _ { \partial M } \alpha dx + \beta dy = \int _ M \left( D _ 1\beta-D _ 2\alpha \right) dx\wedge dy. \]
Apply Stokes' theorem on manifolds to the \(1\)-form \[ \omega=\alpha dx+\beta dy. \] Using the definition of exterior derivative and the antisymmetry of the wedge product, \[ d\omega = D_1\beta\,dx\wedge dy + D_2\alpha\,dy\wedge dx = \left( D_1\beta-D_2\alpha \right)dx\wedge dy. \] Substituting this into Stokes' theorem gives the stated formula.
Divergence Theorem
Let \( M \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ 3 \) be a compact oriented three-dimensional manifold with boundary, and let \( n \) be the outward unit normal on \( \partial M \). If \( F \) is a differentiable vector field on \( M \), then \[ \int _ M \operatorname{div} F\, dV = \int _ { \partial M } \left( F,n \right)dA. \]
Write \( F=\left( P,Q,R \right) \) and associate to \( F \) the \(2\)-form \[ \omega = P\,dy\wedge dz + Q\,dz\wedge dx + R\,dx\wedge dy. \] By the definition of exterior derivative, \[ d\omega = \left( D_1P+D_2Q+D_3R \right) dx\wedge dy\wedge dz = \operatorname{div}F\,dV. \] On the boundary, the pullback of \( \omega \) is \( \left( F,n \right)dA \), by the definition of the outward normal and the surface area element. Applying Stokes' theorem gives the divergence theorem.
Classical Stokes' Theorem
Let \( M \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ 3 \) be a compact oriented surface with boundary, let \( n \) be the unit normal determined by the orientation, and let \( T \) be the positively oriented unit tangent field on \( \partial M \). If \( F \) is a differentiable vector field near \( M \), then \[ \int _ M \left( \nabla \times F,n \right)dA = \int _ { \partial M } \left( F,T \right)ds. \]
Associate to \( F=\left( P,Q,R \right) \) the \(1\)-form \[ \omega=Pdx+Qdy+Rdz. \] Its exterior derivative is the \(2\)-form corresponding to the curl: \[ d\omega = \left( \nabla\times F,n \right)dA \] when restricted to the oriented surface \( M \). On the boundary curve, the pullback of \( \omega \) is \[ \left( F,T \right)ds, \] where \( T \) is the positively oriented unit tangent. Applying Stokes' theorem on manifolds to \( \omega \) gives \[ \int _ M \left( \nabla \times F,n \right)dA = \int _ { \partial M } \left( F,T \right)ds. \]
