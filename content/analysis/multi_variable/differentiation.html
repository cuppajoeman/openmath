Quadratic Function is Differentiable at a Point
Let \( f:\mathbb{ R } ^ 2 \to \mathbb{ R } \) be given by \[ f\left( x,y \right)=x ^ 2+y ^ 2. \] Show that \( f \) is differentiable at \( a=\left( 1,-1 \right) \).
Define \( T:\mathbb{ R } ^ 2\to\mathbb{ R } \) by \[ T\left( h,k \right)=2h-2k. \] This is a linear transformation. For \( \left( x,y \right)\in\mathbb{ R } ^ 2 \), write \[ \left( h,k \right)=\left( x,y \right)-\left( 1,-1 \right)=\left( x-1,y+1 \right). \] Then \[ \begin{aligned} f\left( x,y \right)-\left( f\left( 1,-1 \right)+T\left( h,k \right) \right) &=x ^ 2+y ^ 2-\left( 2+2\left( x-1 \right)-2\left( y+1 \right) \right)\\ &=\left( x-1 \right) ^ 2+\left( y+1 \right) ^ 2\\ &=\left\lVert \left( h,k \right) \right\rVert ^ 2. \end{aligned} \] Therefore \[ \frac{ \left\lvert f\left( x,y \right)-\left( f\left( 1,-1 \right)+T\left( \left( x,y \right)-\left( 1,-1 \right) \right) \right) \right\rvert }{ \left\lVert \left( x,y \right)-\left( 1,-1 \right) \right\rVert } = \left\lVert \left( x,y \right)-\left( 1,-1 \right) \right\rVert. \] As \( \left( x,y \right)\to\left( 1,-1 \right) \), the right side tends to \( 0 \). Hence \( f \) is differentiable at \( \left( 1,-1 \right) \).
Euclidean Norm is Not Differentiable at the Origin
Let \( f:\mathbb{ R } ^ 2 \to \mathbb{ R } \) be the Euclidean norm function \[ f\left( x,y \right)=\left\lVert \left( x,y \right) \right\rVert. \] Show that \( f \) is not differentiable at \( \left( 0,0 \right) \).
Suppose for contradiction that \( f \) is differentiable at \( \left( 0,0 \right) \). Then there exists a linear transformation \( T:\mathbb{ R } ^ 2\to\mathbb{ R } \) such that \[ \lim _ { \left( x,y \right)\to\left( 0,0 \right) } \frac{\left\lvert \left\lVert \left( x,y \right) \right\rVert -T\left( x,y \right)\right\rvert}{\left\lVert \left( x,y \right) \right\rVert} = 0. \] Taking points \( \left( t,0 \right) \) with \( t\gt0 \), we get \[ 0 = \lim _ { t\to0 ^ + } \frac{\left\lvert t-T\left( t,0 \right)\right\rvert}{t} = \left\lvert 1-T\left( 1,0 \right)\right\rvert, \] so \( T\left( 1,0 \right)=1 \). Taking points \( \left( t,0 \right) \) with \( t\lt0 \), we get \[ 0 = \lim _ { t\to0 ^ - } \frac{\left\lvert -t-T\left( t,0 \right)\right\rvert}{-t} = \left\lvert 1+T\left( 1,0 \right)\right\rvert, \] so \( T\left( 1,0 \right)=-1 \). This is impossible. Therefore no such linear transformation \( T \) exists, and \( f \) is not differentiable at \( \left( 0,0 \right) \).

Derivative at a Point
Suppose that \( f: \mathbb{ R } ^ n \to \mathbb{ R } ^ m \) is differentiable at \( a \in \mathbb{ R } ^ n \). The derivative of \( f \) at \( a \), denoted \( Df\left( a \right) \), is the unique linear transformation \( T: \mathbb{ R } ^ n \to \mathbb{ R } ^ m \) from the derivative uniqueness theorem.

Thus \( Df\left( a \right) \) is not a number in general. It is a linear map that takes a small displacement \( h\in\mathbb{ R } ^ n \) and returns the best linear approximation to the change \( f\left( a+h \right)-f\left( a \right) \).

Derivative of the Identity Function
Let \[ \operatorname{id}_{\mathbb R ^ n}:\mathbb R ^ n\to\mathbb R ^ n \] be the identity function. For every \(a\in\mathbb R ^ n\), \[ D\operatorname{id}_{\mathbb R ^ n}\left(a\right) = \operatorname{id}_{\mathbb R ^ n}. \] Equivalently, for every \(v\in\mathbb R ^ n\), \[ D\operatorname{id}_{\mathbb R ^ n}\left(a\right)\left(v\right)=v. \]
Let \(T=\operatorname{id}_{\mathbb R ^ n}\). Then \(T\) is a linear transformation. For every \(h\neq0\), \[ \frac{ \left\| \operatorname{id}_{\mathbb R ^ n}\left(a+h\right) - \operatorname{id}_{\mathbb R ^ n}\left(a\right) - T\left(h\right) \right\| }{ \left\|h\right\| } = \frac{\left\|\left(a+h\right)-a-h\right\|}{\left\|h\right\|} = 0. \] Therefore \(T\) satisfies the defining limit for the derivative of \(\operatorname{id}_{\mathbb R ^ n}\) at \(a\). By derivative uniqueness, \[ D\operatorname{id}_{\mathbb R ^ n}\left(a\right) = \operatorname{id}_{\mathbb R ^ n}. \] Applying both sides to \(v\) gives \(D\operatorname{id}_{\mathbb R ^ n}\left(a\right)\left(v\right)=v\).
Derivative of Squared Norm
Let \[ S:\mathbb R ^ n\to\mathbb R, \qquad S(x)=x\cdot x. \] Show that \(S\) is differentiable at every \(a\in\mathbb R ^ n\), and that for every \(v\in\mathbb R ^ n\), \[ DS\left(a\right)\left(v\right)=2a\cdot v. \]
Fix \(a\in\mathbb R ^ n\). Define \[ T:\mathbb R ^ n\to\mathbb R, \qquad T(h)=2a\cdot h. \] This is a linear transformation. For \(h\in\mathbb R ^ n\), \[ S\left(a+h\right)-S\left(a\right)-T\left(h\right) = \left(a+h\right)\cdot\left(a+h\right)-a\cdot a-2a\cdot h. \] Expanding with the distributivity of the dot product and symmetry of the dot product gives \[ S\left(a+h\right)-S\left(a\right)-T\left(h\right) = h\cdot h. \] Therefore \[ \frac{ \left|S\left(a+h\right)-S\left(a\right)-T\left(h\right)\right| }{ \left\|h\right\| } = \frac{\left|h\cdot h\right|}{\left\|h\right\|} = \left\|h\right\| \to0 \] as \(h\to0\). Thus \(T\) satisfies the defining limit for the derivative of \(S\) at \(a\). By the definition of derivative at a point, \[ DS\left(a\right)=T. \] Applying both sides to \(v\) gives \[ DS\left(a\right)\left(v\right)=2a\cdot v. \]
Jacobian Matrix
If \( f: \mathbb{ R } ^ n \to \mathbb{ R } ^ m \) is differentiable at \( a \), then \( Df\left( a \right) \) is a linear transformation \( \mathbb{ R } ^ n\to\mathbb{ R } ^ m \). Its matrix with respect to the standard bases \( E_n \) and \( E_m \) is called the Jacobian matrix of \( f \) at \( a \), and is denoted \[ \left[ Df\left( a \right) \right] _ { E_m \leftarrow E_n }. \] The notation \( f'\left( a \right) \) is also used for this matrix, but \( Df\left( a \right) \) itself denotes the derivative as a linear transformation.
Jacobian Entries are Partial Derivatives
Let \( f: \mathbb{ R } ^ n \to \mathbb{ R } ^ m \) be differentiable at \( a \), and write \[ f=\left( f ^ 1,\ldots,f ^ m \right). \] Then the \( i,j \)-entry of the Jacobian matrix is the \( j \)-th partial derivative of the \( i \)-th component: \[ \left( \left[ Df\left( a \right) \right] _ { E_m \leftarrow E_n } \right) _ { i,j } = D_j f ^ i\left( a \right). \] Equivalently, \[ \left[ Df\left( a \right) \right] _ { E_m \leftarrow E_n } = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f ^ 1}{\partial x_1}\left( a \right) & \frac{\partial f ^ 1}{\partial x_2}\left( a \right) & \cdots & \frac{\partial f ^ 1}{\partial x_n}\left( a \right) \\ \frac{\partial f ^ 2}{\partial x_1}\left( a \right) & \frac{\partial f ^ 2}{\partial x_2}\left( a \right) & \cdots & \frac{\partial f ^ 2}{\partial x_n}\left( a \right) \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f ^ m}{\partial x_1}\left( a \right) & \frac{\partial f ^ m}{\partial x_2}\left( a \right) & \cdots & \frac{\partial f ^ m}{\partial x_n}\left( a \right) \end{bmatrix}. \]
Let \( T=Df\left( a \right) \). By the standard matrix theorem for linear transformations, the \( j \)-th column of \( \left[ T \right] _ { E_m \leftarrow E_n } \) is \( T\left( e_j \right) \), where \( e_j \) is the \( j \)-th vector in the standard basis of \( \mathbb{ R } ^ n \). Therefore \[ \left( \left[ Df\left( a \right) \right] _ { E_m \leftarrow E_n } \right) _ { i,j } = \left( T\left( e_j \right) \right) _ i. \] Since \( T \) is the derivative of \( f \) at \( a \), \[ f\left( a+t e_j \right) = f\left( a \right)+T\left( t e_j \right)+r\left( t \right), \qquad \frac{\left\lVert r\left( t \right)\right\rVert}{\left\lvert t \right\rvert}\to0 \] as \( t\to0 \). Taking the \( i \)-th component and using the linearity of \( T \), \[ \frac{f ^ i\left( a+t e_j \right)-f ^ i\left( a \right)}{t} = \left( T\left( e_j \right) \right)_i + \frac{r ^ i\left( t \right)}{t}. \] The final term tends to \(0\), so the limit on the left exists and equals \( \left( T\left( e_j \right) \right)_i \). By the definition of partial derivative, that limit is \( D_j f ^ i\left( a \right) \). Hence \[ D_j f ^ i\left( a \right) = \left( \left[ Df\left( a \right) \right] _ { E_m \leftarrow E_n } \right) _ { i,j }. \] Since this holds for every \(i\) and every \(j\), the displayed matrix formula follows.
Differentiability Implies Continuity
If \( f: \mathbb{ R } ^ n \to \mathbb{ R } ^ m \) is differentiable at \( a \), then \( f \) is continuous at \( a \).
Chain Rule for Total Derivatives
Suppose that \( f: \mathbb{ R } ^ n \to \mathbb{ R } ^ m \) is differentiable at \( a \), and \( g: \mathbb{ R } ^ m \to \mathbb{ R } ^ p \) is differentiable at \( f\left( a \right) \). Then the composition \( g \circ f \) is differentiable at \( a \), and \[ D\left( g \circ f \right)\left( a \right) = Dg\left( f\left( a \right) \right) \circ Df\left( a \right). \] Equivalently, \[ \left[ D\left( g\circ f \right)\left( a \right) \right] _ { E_p \leftarrow E_n } = \left[ Dg\left( f\left( a \right) \right) \right] _ { E_p \leftarrow E_m } \left[ Df\left( a \right) \right] _ { E_m \leftarrow E_n }. \]
Basic Derivative Rules in Euclidean Space
The following derivative rules hold:
  • If \( f: \mathbb{ R } ^ n \to \mathbb{ R } ^ m \) is constant, then \( Df\left( a \right) = 0 \).
  • If \( f: \mathbb{ R } ^ n \to \mathbb{ R } ^ m \) is linear, then \( Df\left( a \right) = f \).
  • If \( f: \mathbb{ R } ^ n \to \mathbb{ R } ^ m \), then \( f \) is differentiable at \( a \) if and only if each component \( f ^ i \) is differentiable at \( a \), and \( Df\left( a \right)=\left( Df ^ 1\left( a \right),\ldots,Df ^ m\left( a \right) \right) \).
  • If \( f,g: \mathbb{ R } ^ n \to \mathbb{ R } \) are differentiable at \( a \), then \( D\left( f+g \right)\left( a \right)=Df\left( a \right)+Dg\left( a \right) \).
  • If \( f,g: \mathbb{ R } ^ n \to \mathbb{ R } \) are differentiable at \( a \), then \( D\left( fg \right)\left( a \right)=g\left( a \right)Df\left( a \right)+f\left( a \right)Dg\left( a \right) \).
  • If \( g\left( a \right) \neq 0 \), then \[ D\left( \frac{f}{g} \right)\left( a \right) = \frac{g\left( a \right)Df\left( a \right)-f\left( a \right)Dg\left( a \right)}{g\left( a \right) ^ 2}. \]
Mixed Partials Commute Under Continuity
If \( D _ { i,j } f \) and \( D _ { j,i } f \) are continuous on an open set containing \( a \), then \[ D _ { i,j } f\left( a \right) = D _ { j,i } f\left( a \right). \]
Higher-Order Partial Derivative
A partial derivative of order \(k\) is obtained by applying partial derivative operators \(k\) times in sequence. For example, \[ D _ { i,j } f = D _ i\left( D _ j f \right) \] is a partial derivative of order \(2\).
\( C ^ \infty \) Function
A function is \( C ^ \infty \) if partial derivatives of all orders exist and are continuous.
Interior Extremum has Zero Partials
Let \( A \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ n \). If \( f: A \to \mathbb{ R } \) has a maximum or minimum at a point \( a \) in the interior of \( A \), and \( D _ i f\left( a \right) \) exists, then \[ D _ i f\left( a \right)=0. \]
Matrix Norm
If \( A \) is an \( m \times n \) matrix, its norm is \[ \left\lvert A \right\rvert = \sqrt{\sum _ { i = 1 } ^ m \sum _ { j = 1 } ^ n a _ { i,j } ^ 2 }. \]
Mean Value Inequality in Euclidean Space
Suppose that \( A \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) is open, \( f: A \to \mathbb{ R } ^ m \) is differentiable, and the line segment from \( a \) to \( b \) lies in \( A \). If \( \left\lvert Df\left( x \right)\right\rvert \le M \) for every point \( x \) on that segment, then \[ \left\lvert f\left( b \right)-f\left( a \right)\right\rvert \le M \left\lvert b-a \right\rvert. \]
Continuously Differentiable iff Continuous Partials
A function \( f: \mathbb{ R } ^ n \to \mathbb{ R } ^ m \) is continuously differentiable on an open set \( A \) if and only if all partial derivatives \( D _ j f ^ i \) exist and are continuous on \( A \). In this case the Jacobian matrix is \[ \left[ Df\left( x \right) \right] _ { E_m \leftarrow E_n } = \left( D _ j f ^ i\left( x \right) \right). \]
In this statement, continuously differentiable means that \( f \) is differentiable at every point of \( A \), and that the derivative map \[ x\mapsto Df\left( x \right) \] is continuous when \( Df\left( x \right) \) is represented by its Jacobian matrix.

First suppose \( f \) is continuously differentiable on \( A \). Then \( f \) is differentiable at every \( x\in A \), so \( Df\left( x \right) \) exists. The \( j \)-th partial derivative of the \( i \)-th component function is the \( i,j \)-entry of the Jacobian matrix: \[ D_j f ^ i\left( x \right) = \left( \left[ Df\left( x \right) \right] _ { E_m \leftarrow E_n } \right)_{i,j}. \] Since the matrix-valued function \( x\mapsto \left[ Df\left( x \right) \right] _ { E_m \leftarrow E_n } \) is continuous, each entry function \( x\mapsto D_j f ^ i\left( x \right) \) is continuous. Thus all partial derivatives exist and are continuous on \( A \).

Conversely, suppose all partial derivatives \( D_j f ^ i \) exist and are continuous on \( A \). Fix \( a\in A \). Since \( A \) is open, choose a small rectangle around \( a \) contained in \( A \). Define a linear transformation \( T_a:\mathbb{ R } ^ n\to\mathbb{ R } ^ m \) by \[ T_a h = \left( \sum_{j=1}^n D_j f ^ 1\left( a \right)h_j,\ldots, \sum_{j=1}^n D_j f ^ m\left( a \right)h_j \right). \] We show that \( T_a \) is the derivative of \( f \) at \( a \).

Write \( h=\left( h_1,\ldots,h_n \right) \), and for \( 0\le j\le n \), set \[ z_j=a+\left( h_1,\ldots,h_j,0,\ldots,0 \right). \] For each component \( f ^ i \), telescope the change from \( a \) to \( a+h \): \[ f ^ i\left( a+h \right)-f ^ i\left( a \right) = \sum_{j=1}^n \left( f ^ i\left( z_j \right)-f ^ i\left( z_{j-1} \right) \right). \] Each summand changes only the \( j \)-th coordinate. By the one-variable mean value theorem applied along that coordinate segment, if \( h_j\neq0 \), there is a point \( \xi_j \) on the segment from \( z_{j-1} \) to \( z_j \) such that \[ f ^ i\left( z_j \right)-f ^ i\left( z_{j-1} \right) = D_j f ^ i\left( \xi_j \right)h_j. \] If \( h_j=0 \), the same formula holds with any point \( \xi_j \) on the segment.

Therefore \[ f ^ i\left( a+h \right)-f ^ i\left( a \right) - \sum_{j=1}^n D_j f ^ i\left( a \right)h_j = \sum_{j=1}^n \left( D_j f ^ i\left( \xi_j \right)-D_j f ^ i\left( a \right) \right)h_j. \] Since each \( D_j f ^ i \) is continuous at \( a \), the differences \[ D_j f ^ i\left( \xi_j \right)-D_j f ^ i\left( a \right) \] go to \(0\) as \( h\to0 \). Hence \[ \frac{ \left\lvert f\left( a+h \right)-f\left( a \right)-T_a h\right\rvert }{ \left\lvert h\right\rvert } \to0. \] By the definition of differentiability, \( f \) is differentiable at \( a \), with \( Df\left( a \right)=T_a \).

Since \( a\in A \) was arbitrary, \( f \) is differentiable on \( A \). The matrix of \( T_a \) is exactly \[ \left( D_j f ^ i\left( a \right) \right), \] so \[ \left[ Df\left( a \right) \right] _ { E_m \leftarrow E_n }=\left( D_j f ^ i\left( a \right) \right). \] The entries of this matrix are continuous functions of \( a \), so the Jacobian matrix varies continuously on \( A \). Thus \( f \) is continuously differentiable on \( A \).

Projection Functions are Continuously Differentiable
Each projection function \( \pi ^ i:\mathbb{ R } ^ n\to\mathbb{ R } \) is continuously differentiable.
For the \( i \)-th projection function, \[ D_j\pi ^ i\left( x \right) = \begin{cases} 1 & j=i, \\ 0 & j\neq i. \end{cases} \] These partial derivatives exist and are constant, hence continuous. Therefore \( \pi ^ i \) is continuously differentiable by the continuous partials characterization.
Componentwise Continuously Differentiable Map
Let \( A\subseteq\mathbb{ R } ^ n \) be open. If \( f ^ 1,\ldots,f ^ m:A\to\mathbb{ R } \) are continuously differentiable, then the function \( f:A\to\mathbb{ R } ^ m \) defined by \[ f\left( x \right)=\left( f ^ 1\left( x \right),\ldots,f ^ m\left( x \right) \right) \] is continuously differentiable.
By the definition of component functions, the component functions of \( f \) are \( f ^ 1,\ldots,f ^ m \). Each \( f ^ i \) is continuously differentiable, so all partial derivatives \( D_j f ^ i \) exist and are continuous on \( A \). Therefore \( f \) is continuously differentiable by the continuous partials characterization.
Derivative of the Vector Scalar Division Map
Let \[ Q:\mathbb R ^ m\times\left(\mathbb R\setminus\left\{0\right\}\right)\to\mathbb R ^ m, \qquad Q(y,t)=\frac{y}{t}. \] If \(y\in\mathbb R ^ m\), \(t\neq0\), \(u\in\mathbb R ^ m\), and \(s\in\mathbb R\), then \[ DQ(y,t)(u,s) = \frac{u}{t}-\frac{s}{t ^ 2}y. \]
Write \(y=\left(y_1,\ldots,y_m\right)\). The component functions of \(Q\) are \[ Q ^ i(y,t)=\frac{y_i}{t} \] for \(1\le i\le m\). For \(1\le j\le m\), \[ \frac{\partial Q ^ i}{\partial y_j}(y,t) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{t}, & i=j,\\ 0, & i\neq j, \end{cases} \] and \[ \frac{\partial Q ^ i}{\partial t}(y,t) = -\frac{y_i}{t ^ 2}. \] These partial derivatives are continuous wherever \(t\neq0\). Therefore, by the continuous partials characterization, \(Q\) is differentiable at \((y,t)\).

By the Jacobian entries are partial derivatives corollary, the Jacobian matrix of \(Q\) at \((y,t)\), with input coordinates ordered as \(y_1,\ldots,y_m,t\), is \[ \left[DQ(y,t)\right]_{E_m\leftarrow E_{m+1}} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{t} & 0 & \cdots & 0 & -\frac{y_1}{t ^ 2}\\ 0 & \frac{1}{t} & \cdots & 0 & -\frac{y_2}{t ^ 2}\\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots\\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \frac{1}{t} & -\frac{y_m}{t ^ 2} \end{bmatrix}. \]

Applying this matrix to the displacement \((u,s)=\left(u_1,\ldots,u_m,s\right)\) gives \[ DQ(y,t)(u,s) = \left( \frac{u_1}{t}-\frac{s y_1}{t ^ 2}, \ldots, \frac{u_m}{t}-\frac{s y_m}{t ^ 2} \right) = \frac{u}{t}-\frac{s}{t ^ 2}y. \]
Inverse Function Theorem
Suppose that \( f: \mathbb{ R } ^ n \to \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) is continuously differentiable in an open set containing \( a \), and that the determinant of the Jacobian matrix is nonzero: \[ \det \left[ Df\left( a \right) \right] _ E \neq 0. \] Then there are open sets \( U \) and \( V \) with \( a \in U \), \( f\left( a \right) \in V \), such that \( f: U \to V \) is one-to-one, onto, and has a continuously differentiable inverse. Moreover, \[ \left[ D\left( f ^ { -1 } \right)\left( y \right) \right] _ E = \left( \left[ Df\left( f ^ { -1 }\left( y \right) \right) \right] _ E \right) ^ { -1 }. \] If \( f \) is \( C ^ \infty \), then \( f ^ { -1 } \) is \( C ^ \infty \).
Let \( A=\left[ Df\left( a \right) \right] _ E \). Since \( \det A\neq 0 \), \( A \) is an invertible matrix. Composing \( f \) with the affine change of variables \[ x\mapsto A ^ { -1 }\left( f\left( x \right)-f\left( a \right) \right)+a \] reduces the proof to the case \( f\left( a \right)=a \) and \( \left[ Df\left( a \right) \right] _ E=I_n \). After proving the theorem in this normalized case, composing back with the affine maps gives the original statement.

In the normalized case, continuity of the Jacobian matrix gives a closed rectangle \( R \) with \( a \) in its interior such that \( \left[ Df\left( x \right) \right] _ E \) stays close to \( I_n \) on \( R \). Applying the mean value inequality to \( x\mapsto f\left( x \right)-x \), after shrinking \( R \) if needed, gives \[ \left\lvert \left( f\left( x \right)-x \right) - \left( f\left( y \right)-y \right) \right\rvert \le \frac{1}{2}\left\lvert x-y \right\rvert \] for all \( x,y\in R \). Therefore \[ \left\lvert f\left( x \right)-f\left( y \right)\right\rvert \ge \frac{1}{2}\left\lvert x-y \right\rvert, \] so \( f \) is one-to-one on \( R \).

The boundary of \( R \) is compact, and \( f\left( a \right) \) is not in the image of that boundary. Hence there is an open ball \( V \) around \( f\left( a \right) \) such that every \( z\in V \) is closer to \( f\left( a \right) \) than to any value \( f\left( x \right) \) with \( x \) on the boundary of \( R \).

Fix \( z\in V \), and define \[ \Phi_z\left( x \right)=\left\lvert z-f\left( x \right)\right\rvert ^ 2 \] on \( R \). Since \( R \) is a compact closed rectangle and \( \Phi_z \) is continuous, the extreme value theorem gives a point where \( \Phi_z \) is minimized. The choice of \( V \) prevents this minimum from occurring on the boundary, so the minimum occurs at an interior point \( x \).

By the interior extremum theorem, all partial derivatives of \( \Phi_z \) vanish at \( x \). Written in matrix form, this says \[ \left( z-f\left( x \right) \right)^T \left[ Df\left( x \right) \right] _ E=0. \] Since \( \left[ Df\left( x \right) \right] _ E \) is close to \( I_n \), it is invertible. Therefore \( z-f\left( x \right)=0 \), so \( z=f\left( x \right) \). This proves that every \( z\in V \) has a preimage in the interior of \( R \). Together with injectivity, this gives a local inverse \( f ^ { -1 }:V\to U \), where \( U=f ^ { -1 }\left( V \right)\cap\operatorname{int}\left( R \right) \).

The estimate \[ \left\lvert f\left( x \right)-f\left( y \right)\right\rvert \ge \frac{1}{2}\left\lvert x-y \right\rvert \] implies \[ \left\lvert f ^ { -1 }\left( z_1 \right)-f ^ { -1 }\left( z_2 \right)\right\rvert \le 2\left\lvert z_1-z_2 \right\rvert, \] so \( f ^ { -1 } \) is continuous.

Let \( z=f\left( x \right) \in V \). Since \( f \) is differentiable at \( x \), \[ f\left( x+h \right) = f\left( x \right)+\left[ Df\left( x \right) \right] _ E h+r\left( h \right), \qquad \frac{\left\lvert r\left( h \right)\right\rvert}{\left\lvert h \right\rvert}\to 0. \] Using the continuity estimate for \( f ^ { -1 } \), this remainder identity implies \[ \left[ D\left( f ^ { -1 } \right)\left( z \right) \right] _ E = \left( \left[ Df\left( x \right) \right] _ E \right) ^ { -1 } = \left( \left[ Df\left( f ^ { -1 }\left( z \right) \right) \right] _ E \right) ^ { -1 }. \] Since the Jacobian matrix of \( f \) is continuous and matrix inversion is continuous on invertible matrices, \( f ^ { -1 } \) is continuously differentiable. If \( f \) is \( C ^ \infty \), repeated differentiation of \[ f\left( f ^ { -1 }\left( z \right) \right)=z \] shows inductively that \( f ^ { -1 } \) is \( C ^ \infty \).

Implicit Function Theorem
Suppose that \( F: \mathbb{ R } ^ { n + m } \to \mathbb{ R } ^ m \) is continuously differentiable near \( \left( a,b \right) \), \( F\left( a,b \right)=0 \), and the \( m \times m \) matrix of partial derivatives of \( F \) with respect to the last \( m \) variables is invertible at \( \left( a,b \right) \). Then near \( \left( a,b \right) \), the equation \[ F\left( x,y \right)=0 \] uniquely determines \( y \) as a continuously differentiable function \( y=g\left( x \right) \). If \( F \) is \( C ^ \infty \), then \( g \) is \( C ^ \infty \).
Let \[ B= \left[ \frac{\partial F ^ i}{\partial y_j}\left( a,b \right) \right]_{1\le i,j\le m}. \] By hypothesis, \( B \) is an invertible matrix. Define \[ H:\mathbb{ R } ^ { n+m }\to\mathbb{ R } ^ { n+m }, \qquad H\left( x,y \right)=\left( x,F\left( x,y \right) \right). \] Since \( F \) is continuously differentiable near \( \left( a,b \right) \), there is an open set \( U \) containing \( \left( a,b \right) \) on which \( F \) is continuously differentiable. On \( U \), the first \( n \) component functions of \( H \) are projection functions, and the last \( m \) component functions are the component functions of \( F \). Hence \( H \) is continuously differentiable on \( U \) by the componentwise continuously differentiable map theorem.

The derivative matrix of \( H \) at \( \left( a,b \right) \), written in the \( x \)-variables followed by the \( y \)-variables, has block form \[ H'\left( a,b \right) = \begin{bmatrix} I_n & 0 \\ C & B \end{bmatrix}, \] where \( C \) is the matrix of partial derivatives of \( F \) with respect to the \( x \)-variables. This block matrix is invertible, with inverse \[ \begin{bmatrix} I_n & 0 \\ -B ^ { -1 }C & B ^ { -1 } \end{bmatrix}. \] Indeed, by block matrix multiplication, \[ \begin{bmatrix} I_n & 0 \\ C & B \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} I_n & 0 \\ -B ^ { -1 }C & B ^ { -1 } \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} I_n & 0 \\ C-BB ^ { -1 }C & BB ^ { -1 } \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} I_n & 0 \\ 0 & I_m \end{bmatrix}, \] and the product in the opposite order is also the identity matrix.

By the inverse function theorem, after restricting to open neighborhoods, \( H \) has a continuously differentiable inverse. Write this inverse as \[ H ^ { -1 }\left( s,t \right)=\left( s,k\left( s,t \right) \right). \] The first component must be \( s \), because the first component of \( H\left( x,y \right) \) is \( x \).

Define \[ g\left( s \right)=k\left( s,0 \right). \] Then \[ H\left( s,g\left( s \right) \right) = \left( s,0 \right), \] so \( F\left( s,g\left( s \right) \right)=0 \).

Conversely, if \( F\left( s,y \right)=0 \) for \( \left( s,y \right) \) in the chosen neighborhood, then \[ H\left( s,y \right)=\left( s,0 \right). \] Since \( H \) is one-to-one on that neighborhood, \( \left( s,y \right)=H ^ { -1 }\left( s,0 \right)=\left( s,g\left( s \right) \right) \). Hence \( y=g\left( s \right) \), proving uniqueness.

Since \( g \) is obtained from the continuously differentiable inverse \( H ^ { -1 } \), it is continuously differentiable. If \( F \) is \( C ^ \infty \), then \( H \) is \( C ^ \infty \), so \( H ^ { -1 } \) is \( C ^ \infty \) by the smooth part of the inverse function theorem. Therefore \( g \) is \( C ^ \infty \).

Lagrange Multiplier Condition
If \( g: A \to \mathbb{ R } ^ p \) has full rank \( p \) on \( g ^ { -1 }\left( 0 \right) \), and \( f: A \to \mathbb{ R } \) has a constrained extremum at \( a \in g ^ { -1 }\left( 0 \right) \), then there are scalars \( \lambda _ 1,\ldots,\lambda _ p \) such that \[ df\left( a \right) = \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ p \lambda _ i dg ^ i\left( a \right). \]
