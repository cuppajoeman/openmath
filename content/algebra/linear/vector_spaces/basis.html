Finite Dimensional Vector Space
A vector space \( V \) is said to be finite dimensional if \( \operatorname{ dim } \left( V \right) \) is finite
Standard Basis of Rn
The standard basis of \( \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) is \[ \left\{ e_1, \ldots, e_n \right\} \] where \( e_i \) is the vector whose \( i \)-th coordinate is \(1\) and whose other coordinates are \(0\).
Standard Basis of Rn is a Basis
The standard basis \( \left\{ e_1, \ldots, e_n \right\} \) is a basis of \( \mathbb{ R } ^ n \).
First, the standard basis spans \( \mathbb{ R } ^ n \). If \( x = \left( x_1, \ldots, x_n \right) \in \mathbb{ R } ^ n \), then \[ x = x_1 e_1 + \cdots + x_n e_n \] so \( x \in \operatorname{ span } \left( \left\{ e_1, \ldots, e_n \right\} \right) \) by the definition of span.

Second, the standard basis is linearly independent. If

\[ a_1 e_1 + \cdots + a_n e_n = 0 \] then comparing the \( i \)-th coordinate gives \( a_i = 0 \). Since this holds for every \( i \), all coefficients are zero, so \( \left\{ e_1, \ldots, e_n \right\} \) is linearly independent. Therefore it is a basis by the definition of basis.
Dimension of Rn
\[ \operatorname{ dim } \left( \mathbb{ R } ^ n \right) = n \]
By the standard basis of \( \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) is a basis proposition, \( \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) has a basis with \( n \) elements. Therefore, by the definition of dimension, \( \operatorname{ dim } \left( \mathbb{ R } ^ n \right) = n \).
Linearly Independent Set with Dimension Size is a Basis
Suppose \( V \) is a finite dimensional vector space with \( \operatorname{ dim } \left( V \right) = n \). If \( S \subseteq V \) is linearly independent and \( \left\lvert S \right\rvert = n \), then \( S \) is a basis of \( V \).
By the linear independent set expansion to basis proposition, \( S \) can be extended to a basis \( \mathcal{ B } \) of \( V \). Since \( \operatorname{ dim } \left( V \right) = n \), every basis of \( V \) has \( n \) elements by the definition of dimension. But \( S \) already has \( n \) elements, so the extension cannot add any new vectors. Therefore \( S = \mathcal{ B } \), so \( S \) is a basis of \( V \).
Coordinate Vector in a Basis
Let \(V\) be an \(n\)-dimensional vector space over \(\mathbb R\), and let \(\mathcal B=\left(b_1,\ldots,b_n\right)\) be an ordered basis of \(V\). The coordinate map in the basis \(\mathcal B\) is the function \[ \left[\cdot\right]_{\mathcal B}:V\to\mathbb R^n \] defined as follows. For \(v\in V\), write the unique basis expansion \[ v=\sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i, \] whose existence and uniqueness follow from basis implies unique representation. Then the coordinate vector of \(v\) in the basis \(\mathcal B\) is \[ \left[v\right]_{\mathcal B} = \begin{bmatrix} a_1\\ \vdots\\ a_n \end{bmatrix} \in\mathbb R^n. \]

The older notation \( \text{wam}\left( v,\mathcal{B} \right) \) means the same thing. The inverse operation takes a coordinate column and reconstructs the vector in \( V \).

A small subtlety appears when \(V=\mathbb R^n\). Since \(\mathbb R^n\) is an \(n\)-dimensional vector space, any ordered basis \(\mathcal X=\left(x_1,\ldots,x_n\right)\) of \(\mathbb R^n\) gives a coordinate map \[ \left[\cdot\right]_{\mathcal X}:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^n. \] If \(v=\left(v_1,\ldots,v_n\right)\in\mathbb R^n\), then \(\left[v\right]_{\mathcal X}\) is the vector of coefficients \[ \left[v\right]_{\mathcal X} = \begin{bmatrix} c_1\\ \vdots\\ c_n \end{bmatrix} = \left(c_1,\ldots,c_n\right) \in\mathbb R^n \] such that \[ c_1x_1+\cdots+c_nx_n = \left(v_1,\ldots,v_n\right). \] Thus, in this special case, the coordinate map takes an element of \(\mathbb R^n\) and returns an element of \(\mathbb R^n\), but that is a coincidence of the example. The vector \(\left[v\right]_{\mathcal X}\) is an encoding: its entries are the coefficients placed on the basis vectors \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\) so that the resulting linear combination equals \(v\). It also happens to be an element of \(\mathbb R^n\), just as \(v\) is. So even though \(v\) and \(\left[v\right]_{\mathcal X}\) both live in \(\mathbb R^n\), they are being read differently: \(v\) is the vector being represented, while \(\left[v\right]_{\mathcal X}\) is the coefficient list that represents it in the basis \(\mathcal X\). This is why it is often easier to first learn coordinate vectors in a vector space \(V\) that is not literally \(\mathbb R^n\); then the difference between a vector and its coordinate representation is harder to blur.

Coordinate Vector in a Nonstandard Basis
Let \(\mathcal E=\left(e_1,e_2\right)\) be the standard basis of \(\mathbb R^2\), and let \[ \mathcal A = \left( 2e_1+e_2,\, e_1+3e_2 \right). \] If \[ v=7e_1+11e_2, \] find \(\left[v\right]_{\mathcal A}\).
We need scalars \(a_1,a_2\in\mathbb R\) such that \[ v = a_1\left(2e_1+e_2\right) + a_2\left(e_1+3e_2\right). \] Expanding in the standard basis gives \[ 7e_1+11e_2 = \left(2a_1+a_2\right)e_1 + \left(a_1+3a_2\right)e_2. \] Since the standard basis representation is unique, the coefficients satisfy \[ 2a_1+a_2=7, \qquad a_1+3a_2=11. \] Solving gives \(a_1=2\) and \(a_2=3\). Therefore \[ \left[v\right]_{\mathcal A} = \begin{bmatrix} 2\\ 3 \end{bmatrix}. \]

Change of Basis Matrix
Let \(V\) be an \(n\)-dimensional vector space over \(\mathbb R\), and let \[ \mathcal A=\left(a_1,\ldots,a_n\right), \qquad \mathcal B=\left(b_1,\ldots,b_n\right) \] be ordered bases of \(V\). The change of basis matrix from \(\mathcal A\) to \(\mathcal B\), denoted \[ [\mathcal B\leftarrow\mathcal A], \] is the matrix satisfying \[ [\mathcal B\leftarrow\mathcal A]\left[x\right]_{\mathcal A} = \left[x\right]_{\mathcal B} \] for every \(x\in V\).
Change of Basis Matrix Columns
Let \(\mathcal A=\left(a_1,\ldots,a_n\right)\) and \(\mathcal B=\left(b_1,\ldots,b_n\right)\) be ordered bases of an \(n\)-dimensional vector space \(V\). Then \[ [\mathcal B\leftarrow\mathcal A] = \begin{bmatrix} \uparrow & \uparrow & & \uparrow\\ \left[a_1\right]_{\mathcal B} & \left[a_2\right]_{\mathcal B} & \cdots & \left[a_n\right]_{\mathcal B}\\ \downarrow & \downarrow & & \downarrow \end{bmatrix}. \] That is, the \(j\)-th column of \([\mathcal B\leftarrow\mathcal A]\) is the \(\mathcal B\)-coordinate vector of the \(j\)-th basis vector of \(\mathcal A\).
Let \(e_j\) be the \(j\)-th vector in the standard basis \(\mathcal E\) of \(\mathbb R^n\). Since \(a_j\) is the \(j\)-th vector of the basis \(\mathcal A\), \[ \left[a_j\right]_{\mathcal A}=e_j. \] By the definition of the change of basis matrix, \[ [\mathcal B\leftarrow\mathcal A]\left[a_j\right]_{\mathcal A} = \left[a_j\right]_{\mathcal B}. \] Substituting \(\left[a_j\right]_{\mathcal A}=e_j\) gives \[ [\mathcal B\leftarrow\mathcal A]e_j = \left[a_j\right]_{\mathcal B}. \] By matrix times standard basis vector is a column, \([\mathcal B\leftarrow\mathcal A]e_j\) is the \(j\)-th column of \([\mathcal B\leftarrow\mathcal A]\). Therefore the \(j\)-th column is \(\left[a_j\right]_{\mathcal B}\). Since this holds for every \(j\in\left\{1,\ldots,n\right\}\), the matrix is exactly \[ \begin{bmatrix} \uparrow & \uparrow & & \uparrow\\ \left[a_1\right]_{\mathcal B} & \left[a_2\right]_{\mathcal B} & \cdots & \left[a_n\right]_{\mathcal B}\\ \downarrow & \downarrow & & \downarrow \end{bmatrix}. \]
