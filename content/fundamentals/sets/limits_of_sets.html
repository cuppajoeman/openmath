Increasing Sequence of Sets
Let \(\left(A_n\right)\) be a sequence of sets. The sequence \(\left(A_n\right)\) is increasing if \[ A_1 \subseteq A_2 \subseteq A_3 \subseteq \cdots . \]
Decreasing Sequence of Sets
Let \(\left(A_n\right)\) be a sequence of sets. The sequence \(\left(A_n\right)\) is decreasing if \[ A_1 \supseteq A_2 \supseteq A_3 \supseteq \cdots . \]
Sequence of Sets Increases to a Set
Let \(\left(A_n\right)\) be an increasing sequence of sets. We say that \(\left(A_n\right)\) increases to \(A\), and write \(A_n \nearrow A\), if \[ A = \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n. \]
Sequence of Sets Decreases to a Set
Let \(\left(A_n\right)\) be a decreasing sequence of sets. We say that \(\left(A_n\right)\) decreases to \(A\), and write \(A_n \searrow A\), if \[ A = \bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n. \]

The intervals \(A_n=\left[-1/n,1/n\right]\) form a decreasing sequence of sets, and \(A_n \searrow \left\{0\right\}\). Each later interval is contained in the previous one, and the only point that remains in every interval is \(0\).

Sequence of Sets Shrinks to a Point
Let \(X\) be a set, let \(p\in X\), and let \(\left(A_n\right)\) be a sequence of subsets of \(X\). We say that \(\left(A_n\right)\) shrinks to \(p\) if \(A_n \searrow \left\{p\right\}\).

This notation is useful when a quantity is first measured on a region and then localized to one point. For instance, instead of writing an informal limit like \(A\to p\), one can choose sets \(A_n\) with \(A_n \searrow \left\{p\right\}\) and then take an ordinary sequence limit as \(n\to\infty\).
