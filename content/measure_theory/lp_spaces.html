\( L ^ p \)-Norm
Suppose that \( \left( X, \mathcal{ S }, \mu \right) \) is a measure space, \( p \in [1,\infty) \), and \( f: X \to \mathbb{ F } \) is measurable. Define \[ \lVert f \rVert _ p := \left( \int _ X \left\lvert f \right\rvert ^ p \, d\mu \right) ^ { 1/p }. \]
\( L ^ p \)-Space
Suppose that \( p \in [1,\infty) \). The \( L ^ p \)-space of \( \mu \) is the vector space of equivalence classes of measurable functions \( f: X \to \mathbb{ F } \) such that \( \lVert f \rVert _ p \lt \infty \), where functions equal almost everywhere are identified.
Essential Supremum Norm
Suppose that \( f: X \to \mathbb{ F } \) is measurable. Define \[ \lVert f \rVert _ \infty := \inf \left\{ c \in [0,\infty] : \left\lvert f \left( x \right) \right\rvert \le c \text{ for almost every } x \in X \right\}. \]
\( L ^ \infty \)-Space
The \( L ^ \infty \)-space of \( \mu \) is the vector space of equivalence classes of measurable functions \( f: X \to \mathbb{ F } \) such that \( \lVert f \rVert _ \infty \lt \infty \), where functions equal almost everywhere are identified.
Holder's Inequality
Suppose that \( p,q \in [1,\infty] \) and \( 1/p + 1/q = 1 \). If \( f \in L ^ p \left( \mu \right) \) and \( g \in L ^ q \left( \mu \right) \), then \( fg \in L ^ 1 \left( \mu \right) \) and \[ \lVert fg \rVert _ 1 \le \lVert f \rVert _ p \lVert g \rVert _ q . \]

If \( \lVert f\rVert _ p=0 \) or \( \lVert g\rVert _ q=0 \), the result is immediate after modifying on a null set. Otherwise normalize so \( \lVert f\rVert _ p=\lVert g\rVert _ q=1 \). Young's inequality gives \( ab\le a ^ p/p+b ^ q/q \) for \( a,b\ge0 \), so \[ \left\lvert f g\right\rvert \le \frac{\left\lvert f\right\rvert ^ p}{p} + \frac{\left\lvert g\right\rvert ^ q}{q}. \] Integrating and using the definition of the integral gives \( \lVert fg\rVert _ 1\le 1 \). Rescaling proves the stated inequality.

Minkowski's Inequality
Suppose that \( p \in [1,\infty] \). If \( f,g \in L ^ p \left( \mu \right) \), then \[ \lVert f + g \rVert _ p \le \lVert f \rVert _ p + \lVert g \rVert _ p . \]

For \( 1\lt p\lt\infty \), apply Holder's inequality with conjugate exponent \( q \) to \[ \left\lvert f+g\right\rvert ^ p \le \left\lvert f\right\rvert \left\lvert f+g\right\rvert ^ {p-1} + \left\lvert g\right\rvert \left\lvert f+g\right\rvert ^ {p-1}. \] This gives \( \lVert f+g\rVert _ p ^ p\le \left( \lVert f\rVert _ p+\lVert g\rVert _ p\right)\lVert f+g\rVert _ p ^ {p-1} \), and division yields the result. The cases \( p=1 \) and \( p=\infty \) follow directly from the triangle inequality for absolute value and the essential supremum norm.

\( L ^ p \) is Complete
Suppose that \( p \in [1,\infty] \). Then \( L ^ p \left( \mu \right) \), with its \( L ^ p \)-norm, is a Banach space.

Let \( \left( f _ n\right) \) be Cauchy in \( L ^ p \). Choose a subsequence with \( \lVert f _ { n_{k+1}}-f _ { n_k}\rVert _ p\lt 2^{-k} \). The series \( \sum _ k \left\lvert f _ { n_{k+1}}-f _ { n_k}\right\rvert \) has finite \( L ^ p \)-norm by Minkowski's inequality and the monotone convergence theorem, so it is finite almost everywhere. Thus the subsequence converges pointwise almost everywhere to a measurable \( f \). Another application of Fatou's lemma gives \( \lVert f _ { n_k}-f\rVert _ p\to0 \), and the original Cauchy sequence then converges to \( f \). Hence \( L ^ p \) is complete.

Density of Simple Functions in \( L ^ p \)
Suppose that \( p \in [1,\infty) \). The simple functions in \( L ^ p \left( \mu \right) \) are dense in \( L ^ p \left( \mu \right) \).

For \( f\in L ^ p\left( \mu\right) \), the function \( \left\lvert f\right\rvert ^ p \) is in \( L ^ 1\left( \mu\right) \). By approximation by simple functions, choose simple \( s_n \to f \) pointwise with \( \left\lvert s_n\right\rvert \le \left\lvert f\right\rvert \). Then \( \left\lvert f-s_n\right\rvert ^ p\le 2^p\left\lvert f\right\rvert ^p \), and the dominated convergence theorem gives \( \lVert f-s_n\rVert _ p\to0 \).

Density of Continuous Compactly Supported Functions in \( L ^ p \)
Suppose that \( p \in [1,\infty) \). Continuous compactly supported functions are dense in \( L ^ p \left( \mathbb{ R } \right) \).

By density of simple functions, approximate \( f \) in \( L ^ p \) by a finite linear combination of characteristic functions of finite-measure Lebesgue measurable sets. The regularity characterization lets us approximate those sets by finite unions of intervals. Replacing interval characteristic functions by continuous tent functions with compact support changes the \( L ^ p \)-norm by an arbitrarily small amount. Combining the finitely many errors proves density.

Riesz-Fischer Theorem
Suppose that \( p \in [1,\infty] \). Every Cauchy sequence in \( L ^ p \left( \mu \right) \) converges in \( L ^ p \left( \mu \right) \).

This is the sequential form of \( L ^ p \) completeness. In a normed space, completeness means precisely that every Cauchy sequence converges in the norm, so the theorem follows immediately.
