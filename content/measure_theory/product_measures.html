Product \( \sigma \)-Algebra
Suppose that \( \left( X, \mathcal{ S } \right) \) and \( \left( Y, \mathcal{ T } \right) \) are measurable spaces. The product \( \sigma \)-algebra \( \mathcal{ S } \otimes \mathcal{ T } \) is the smallest \( \sigma \)-algebra on \( X \times Y \) containing every rectangle \( A \times B \) with \( A \in \mathcal{ S } \) and \( B \in \mathcal{ T } \).
Measurable Rectangle
Suppose that \( \left( X, \mathcal{ S } \right) \) and \( \left( Y, \mathcal{ T } \right) \) are measurable spaces. A measurable rectangle in \( X \times Y \) is a set of the form \( A \times B \), where \( A \in \mathcal{ S } \) and \( B \in \mathcal{ T } \).
Section of a Set
Suppose that \( E \subseteq X \times Y \). For \( x \in X \), define \[ E _ x := \left\{ y \in Y : \left( x, y \right) \in E \right\}. \] For \( y \in Y \), define \[ E ^ y := \left\{ x \in X : \left( x, y \right) \in E \right\}. \]
Sections of Measurable Sets are Measurable
Suppose that \( E \in \mathcal{ S } \otimes \mathcal{ T } \). Then \( E _ x \in \mathcal{ T } \) for every \( x \in X \), and \( E ^ y \in \mathcal{ S } \) for every \( y \in Y \).
Product Measure
Suppose that \( \left( X, \mathcal{ S }, \mu \right) \) and \( \left( Y, \mathcal{ T }, \nu \right) \) are \( \sigma \)-finite measure spaces. The product measure \( \mu \times \nu \) is the measure on \( \mathcal{ S } \otimes \mathcal{ T } \) satisfying \[ \left( \mu \times \nu \right) \left( A \times B \right) = \mu \left( A \right) \nu \left( B \right) \] for every \( A \in \mathcal{ S } \) and \( B \in \mathcal{ T } \).
Tonelli's Theorem
Suppose that \( \left( X, \mathcal{ S }, \mu \right) \) and \( \left( Y, \mathcal{ T }, \nu \right) \) are \( \sigma \)-finite measure spaces and \( f: X \times Y \to [0,\infty] \) is \( \mathcal{ S } \otimes \mathcal{ T } \)-measurable. Then the section functions are measurable and \[ \int _ { X \times Y } f \, d\left( \mu \times \nu \right) = \int _ X \left( \int _ Y f \left( x,y \right) \, d\nu \left( y \right) \right) d\mu \left( x \right) = \int _ Y \left( \int _ X f \left( x,y \right) \, d\mu \left( x \right) \right) d\nu \left( y \right). \]
Fubini's Theorem
Suppose that \( \left( X, \mathcal{ S }, \mu \right) \) and \( \left( Y, \mathcal{ T }, \nu \right) \) are \( \sigma \)-finite measure spaces and \( f \in L ^ 1 \left( \mu \times \nu \right) \). Then \( f \left( x,\cdot \right) \in L ^ 1 \left( \nu \right) \) for almost every \( x \), \( f \left( \cdot,y \right) \in L ^ 1 \left( \mu \right) \) for almost every \( y \), and the iterated integrals exist and are equal to \( \int f \, d\left( \mu \times \nu \right) \).
Lebesgue Measure on Products
Lebesgue measure on \( \mathbb{ R } ^ { m+n } \) agrees with the product of Lebesgue measure on \( \mathbb{ R } ^ m \) and Lebesgue measure on \( \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) on product-measurable sets.
Change of Variables for Lebesgue Integrals
Suppose that \( U,V \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) are open and \( \Phi: U \to V \) is a continuously differentiable bijection with continuously differentiable inverse. If \( f: V \to [0,\infty] \) is measurable, then \[ \int _ V f \, d\lambda = \int _ U f \left( \Phi \left( x \right) \right) \left\lvert \det D\Phi \left( x \right) \right\rvert \, d\lambda \left( x \right). \]
