Product \( \sigma \)-Algebra
Suppose that \( \left( X, \mathcal{ S } \right) \) and \( \left( Y, \mathcal{ T } \right) \) are measurable spaces. The product \( \sigma \)-algebra \( \mathcal{ S } \otimes \mathcal{ T } \) is the smallest \( \sigma \)-algebra on \( X \times Y \) containing every rectangle \( A \times B \) with \( A \in \mathcal{ S } \) and \( B \in \mathcal{ T } \).
Measurable Rectangle
Suppose that \( \left( X, \mathcal{ S } \right) \) and \( \left( Y, \mathcal{ T } \right) \) are measurable spaces. A measurable rectangle in \( X \times Y \) is a set of the form \( A \times B \), where \( A \in \mathcal{ S } \) and \( B \in \mathcal{ T } \).
Section of a Set
Suppose that \( E \subseteq X \times Y \). For \( x \in X \), define \[ E _ x := \left\{ y \in Y : \left( x, y \right) \in E \right\}. \] For \( y \in Y \), define \[ E ^ y := \left\{ x \in X : \left( x, y \right) \in E \right\}. \]
Sections of Measurable Sets are Measurable
Suppose that \( E \in \mathcal{ S } \otimes \mathcal{ T } \). Then \( E _ x \in \mathcal{ T } \) for every \( x \in X \), and \( E ^ y \in \mathcal{ S } \) for every \( y \in Y \).

Let \( \mathcal{ A} \) be the class of sets \( E\subseteq X\times Y \) whose \( x \)-sections are in \( \mathcal{ T} \) and whose \( y \)-sections are in \( \mathcal{ S} \). Rectangles \( A\times B \) with \( A\in\mathcal{ S} \) and \( B\in\mathcal{ T} \) belong to \( \mathcal{ A} \). The class \( \mathcal{ A} \) is a \( \sigma \)-algebra, because sections commute with complements and countable unions. Therefore \( \mathcal{ S}\otimes\mathcal{ T}\subseteq\mathcal{ A} \), proving the claim.

Product Measure
Suppose that \( \left( X, \mathcal{ S }, \mu \right) \) and \( \left( Y, \mathcal{ T }, \nu \right) \) are \( \sigma \)-finite measure spaces. The product measure \( \mu \times \nu \) is the measure on \( \mathcal{ S } \otimes \mathcal{ T } \) satisfying \[ \left( \mu \times \nu \right) \left( A \times B \right) = \mu \left( A \right) \nu \left( B \right) \] for every \( A \in \mathcal{ S } \) and \( B \in \mathcal{ T } \).
Tonelli's Theorem
Suppose that \( \left( X, \mathcal{ S }, \mu \right) \) and \( \left( Y, \mathcal{ T }, \nu \right) \) are \( \sigma \)-finite measure spaces and \( f: X \times Y \to [0,\infty] \) is \( \mathcal{ S } \otimes \mathcal{ T } \)-measurable. Then the section functions are measurable and \[ \int _ { X \times Y } f \, d\left( \mu \times \nu \right) = \int _ X \left( \int _ Y f \left( x,y \right) \, d\nu \left( y \right) \right) d\mu \left( x \right) = \int _ Y \left( \int _ X f \left( x,y \right) \, d\mu \left( x \right) \right) d\nu \left( y \right). \]

First prove the formula for characteristic functions of measurable rectangles, where both sides equal \( \mu\left( A\right)\nu\left( B\right) \). By finite linearity, it holds for nonnegative simple functions. For a nonnegative measurable \( f \), choose increasing simple functions converging to \( f \) by approximation by simple functions. The section functions are measurable by the same simple-function approximation and measurable sections. Passing to the limit with the monotone convergence theorem gives both iterated-integral identities.

Fubini's Theorem
Suppose that \( \left( X, \mathcal{ S }, \mu \right) \) and \( \left( Y, \mathcal{ T }, \nu \right) \) are \( \sigma \)-finite measure spaces and \( f \in L ^ 1 \left( \mu \times \nu \right) \). Then \( f \left( x,\cdot \right) \in L ^ 1 \left( \nu \right) \) for almost every \( x \), \( f \left( \cdot,y \right) \in L ^ 1 \left( \mu \right) \) for almost every \( y \), and the iterated integrals exist and are equal to \( \int f \, d\left( \mu \times \nu \right) \).

Apply Tonelli's theorem to \( \left\lvert f\right\rvert \). Since \( f\in L ^ 1\left( \mu\times\nu\right) \), the iterated integrals of \( \left\lvert f\right\rvert \) are finite, so the sections are integrable for almost every parameter. Decompose \( f=f ^ +-f^- \), apply Tonelli to \( f ^ + \) and \( f^- \), and subtract the two finite identities to obtain the Fubini formula.

Lebesgue Measure on Products
Lebesgue measure on \( \mathbb{ R } ^ { m+n } \) agrees with the product of Lebesgue measure on \( \mathbb{ R } ^ m \) and Lebesgue measure on \( \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) on product-measurable sets.

Both sides are measures on the product \( \sigma \)-algebra and agree on rectangles, since the Lebesgue measure of a rectangle is the product of the side measures. Rectangles form a generating \( \pi \)-system for the product \( \sigma \)-algebra, and the measures are \( \sigma \)-finite on countable unions of bounded rectangles. The uniqueness part of the product-measure construction therefore implies that the two measures agree on all product-measurable sets.

Measure Transformation under a Diffeomorphism
Suppose that \( U,V \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) are open, \( \Phi: U \to V \) is a continuously differentiable bijection with continuously differentiable inverse, and \(\lambda\) is Lebesgue measure on \(\mathbb R^n\). If \(E\subseteq U\) is measurable, then \[ \lambda\left(\Phi(E)\right) = \int_E \left\lvert \det \left[ D\Phi \left( x \right) \right] _ E \right\rvert \,d\lambda(x). \]

Start with an invertible linear map \(T:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^n\). For elementary linear maps the claim is direct: coordinate permutations and orthogonal maps preserve Euclidean volume, a shear preserves the volume of boxes, and scaling one coordinate by \(c\neq0\) multiplies volume by \(\left|c\right|\). Every invertible matrix is a product of these elementary maps, so for every measurable \(E\subseteq\mathbb R^n\),

\[ \lambda\left(T(E)\right) = \left|\det [T]_E\right|\lambda(E). \]

Now let \(\Phi:U\to V\) be a \(C^1\)-diffeomorphism. First work on a compact rectangle \(K\subseteq U\). Since \(x\mapsto [D\Phi(x)]_E\) is continuous, it is uniformly continuous on \(K\). Therefore, after subdividing \(K\) into sufficiently small cubes, \([D\Phi(x)]_E\) changes very little on each cube.

Fix one such cube \(C\) and choose \(a\in C\). By differentiability,

\[ \Phi(x) = \Phi(a)+D\Phi(a)(x-a)+R_a(x), \qquad \frac{\left\lvert R_a(x)\right\rvert}{\left\lvert x-a\right\rvert}\to0. \]

On a small cube this says that \(\Phi(C)\) is squeezed between small enlargements of the affine image

\[ \Phi(a)+D\Phi(a)(C-a). \]

By the linear case, that affine image has Lebesgue measure

\[ \left|\det\left[D\Phi(a)\right]_E\right|\lambda(C). \]

Because the determinant is continuous and \([D\Phi(x)]_E\) varies little on \(C\), this differs only slightly from

\[ \int_C \left|\det\left[D\Phi(x)\right]_E\right| \,d\lambda(x). \]

Summing over the cubes in the subdivision and refining the subdivision gives

\[ \lambda\left(\Phi(K)\right) = \int_K \left|\det\left[D\Phi(x)\right]_E\right| \,d\lambda(x). \]

For a general measurable \(E\subseteq U\), write \(E\) as an increasing union of bounded measurable pieces whose closures lie in \(U\), up to a null remainder. Applying the compact-rectangle result to these pieces and passing to the limit with the monotone convergence theorem gives the displayed measure transformation formula for \(E\).

Change of Variables for Lebesgue Integrals
Suppose that \( U,V \subseteq \mathbb{ R } ^ n \) are open, \( \Phi: U \to V \) is a continuously differentiable bijection with continuously differentiable inverse, and \(\lambda\) is Lebesgue measure on \(\mathbb R^n\). If \( f: V \to [0,\infty] \) is measurable, then \[ \int _ V f \, d\lambda = \int _ U f \left( \Phi \left( x \right) \right) \left\lvert \det \left[ D\Phi \left( x \right) \right] _ E \right\rvert \, d\lambda \left( x \right). \]
The integral formula follows from measure transformation under a diffeomorphism and the construction of the Lebesgue integral.

First suppose that \(f=\chi_B\) is the characteristic function of a measurable set \(B\subseteq V\). Apply measure transformation under a diffeomorphism to \(E=\Phi^{-1}(B)\). Then

\[ \int_V \chi_B\,d\lambda = \lambda(B) = \lambda\left(\Phi\left(\Phi^{-1}(B)\right)\right) = \int_U \chi_B\left(\Phi(x)\right) \left|\det\left[D\Phi(x)\right]_E\right| \,d\lambda(x). \]

By finite additivity and linearity, the same formula holds for every nonnegative simple function on \(V\). Finally, if \(f:V\to[0,\infty]\) is measurable, choose an increasing sequence of nonnegative simple functions \(s_k\) with \(s_k\to f\) pointwise by approximation by simple functions. Applying the simple-function case to each \(s_k\) and passing to the limit on both sides by the monotone convergence theorem proves the change of variables formula for \(f\).
