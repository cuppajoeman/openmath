Simple Random Walk

Simple Random Walk
Let \( X _ 1,X _ 2,\ldots \) be independent random variables with \[ P \left( X _ i = 1 \right)=p, \qquad P \left( X _ i = -1 \right)=1-p. \] The process \( S _ 0 = a \) and \[ S _ n = a + \sum _ { k=1 } ^ n X _ k \] is a simple random walk starting at \( a \).
Symmetric Simple Random Walk
A simple random walk is symmetric if \( p=\frac{1}{2} \).
Spatial Homogeneity
For a simple random walk, \[ P \left( S _ { n+1 } = y+1 \mid S _ n = y \right)=p, \qquad P \left( S _ { n+1 } = y-1 \mid S _ n = y \right)=1-p, \] and these probabilities do not depend on \( y \).
The next position is \( S _ { n+1 }=S _ n+X _ { n+1 } \), and \( X _ { n+1 } \) has the same distribution at every site.
Temporal Homogeneity
For a simple random walk, the one-step transition probabilities do not depend on the time \( n \).
The increments \( X _ 1,X _ 2,\ldots \) are identically distributed.
Markov Property
A simple random walk satisfies \[ P \left( S _ { n+1 }=z \mid S _ 0,\ldots,S _ n \right) = P \left( S _ { n+1 }=z \mid S _ n \right). \]
Once \( S _ n \) is known, the next state is determined by the fresh increment \( X _ { n+1 } \), which is independent of the previous increments.
Position Distribution
If \( S _ 0=0 \) for a simple random walk, then \[ P \left( S _ n = k \right) = \binom{n}{\frac{n+k}{2}} p ^ { \frac{n+k}{2} } \left( 1-p \right) ^ { \frac{n-k}{2} } \] whenever \( n+k \) is even and \( \left\lvert k \right\rvert \le n \). Otherwise \( P \left( S _ n=k \right)=0 \).
To end at \( k \), the walk must make \( u \) upward steps and \( d \) downward steps with \( u+d=n \) and \( u-d=k \). Thus \( u=\frac{n+k}{2} \) and \( d=\frac{n-k}{2} \). Choose which \( u \) of the \( n \) increments are upward.
Reflection Principle
For a symmetric simple random walk starting at \( 0 \), paths from \( 0 \) to \( b \gt 0 \) that hit level \( a \gt 0 \) before time \( n \) are in bijection with paths from \( 0 \) to \( 2a-b \) in \( n \) steps.
Reflect the portion of the path after its first visit to \( a \). This changes the endpoint from \( b \) to \( 2a-b \), and applying the same reflection again recovers the original path.
Gamblers Ruin Probability
Let \( S _ n \) be a simple random walk on \( \left\{ 0,1,\ldots,N \right\} \) stopped when it hits \( 0 \) or \( N \), and suppose \( S _ 0=i \). If \( p \neq \frac{1}{2} \), then \[ P _ i \left( \text{hit } N \text{ before } 0 \right) = \frac{1-\left( \frac{1-p}{p} \right) ^ i} {1-\left( \frac{1-p}{p} \right) ^ N}. \] If \( p=\frac{1}{2} \), then this probability is \( \frac{i}{N} \).
Let \( h \left( i \right) \) be the probability of hitting \( N \) before \( 0 \) from \( i \). Then \( h \left( 0 \right)=0 \), \( h \left( N \right)=1 \), and \[ h \left( i \right) = p h \left( i+1 \right) + \left( 1-p \right)h \left( i-1 \right). \] Solving this second-order difference equation gives the displayed formula; in the symmetric case the solution is linear.
Symmetric Gamblers Ruin Bankruptcy Probability
In the symmetric case, a walk starting at \( i \in \left\{ 0,\ldots,N \right\} \) hits \( 0 \) before \( N \) with probability \[ 1 - \frac{i}{N}. \]
This is the complement of the probability of hitting \( N \) before \( 0 \).
