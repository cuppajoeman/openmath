Sample Space
The sample space of an experiment is the set \( \Omega \) whose elements are the possible outcomes of the experiment.
Event
An event is a subset of the sample space. If the realized outcome \( \omega \in \Omega \) lies in \( A \subseteq \Omega \), then the event \( A \) occurs.
Impossible and Certain Events
The empty set \( \emptyset \) is the impossible event, and the whole sample space \( \Omega \) is the certain event.
Increasing Sequence of Events
A sequence of events \( A _ 1,A _ 2,\ldots \) is increasing if \[ A _ 1 \subseteq A _ 2 \subseteq A _ 3 \subseteq \cdots . \]
Decreasing Sequence of Events
A sequence of events \( A _ 1,A _ 2,\ldots \) is decreasing if \[ A _ 1 \supseteq A _ 2 \supseteq A _ 3 \supseteq \cdots . \]
Event Sequence Limits
If \( A _ 1,A _ 2,\ldots \) is an increasing sequence of events and \( A = \bigcup _ { n=1 } ^ \infty A _ n \), then we say that \( A _ n \) increases to \( A \) and write \( A _ n \nearrow A \).

If \( A _ 1,A _ 2,\ldots \) is a decreasing sequence of events and \( A = \bigcap _ { n=1 } ^ \infty A _ n \), then we say that \( A _ n \) decreases to \( A \) and write \( A _ n \searrow A \).

Probability Measure
Let \( \mathcal{ F } \) be a \( \sigma \)-algebra on \( \Omega \). A function \( P: \mathcal{ F } \to \left[ 0,1 \right] \) is a probability measure if \( P \left( \Omega \right) = 1 \) and, for every pairwise disjoint sequence \( A _ 1,A _ 2,\ldots \in \mathcal{ F } \), \[ P \left( \bigcup _ { n=1 } ^ \infty A _ n \right) = \sum _ { n=1 } ^ \infty P \left( A _ n \right). \]
Probability Space
A probability space is a triple \( \left( \Omega,\mathcal{ F },P \right) \), where \( \Omega \) is a sample space, \( \mathcal{ F } \) is a \( \sigma \)-algebra of events, and \( P \) is a probability measure on \( \mathcal{ F } \).
Finite Uniform Distribution
For any specific \( N \in \mathbb{N} \) , the random variable \( X \) is said to have a (finite discrete) uniform distribution on the sample space \( \Omega=\{1, \ldots, N\} \) - denoted: \( X \sim \) unif \(\{1, \ldots, N\}\) iff \[ P(X=k)=1 / N, \quad k=1, \ldots, N . \]
Conditional Probability
Let \( A, B \subseteq \Omega \) be events, with \( P \left( B \right) \neq 0 \). The conditional probability of \( A \) given \( B \) is defined as \[ P \left( A \mid B \right) = \frac{P \left( A \cap B \right)}{P \left( B \right)}. \]
Intersection as Conditional Probability
Suppose that \( P \left( A \right) , P \left( B \right) \neq 0 \). By the definition of conditional probability, \[ P \left( A \cap B \right) = P \left( A \mid B \right) P \left( B \right) \]
Finite Intersection as Conditional Probability
Suppose we have events \( E _ 1, \ldots , E _ n \). Then \[ P \left( E _ 1 \cap E _ 2 \cap \cdots \cap E _ n \right) = P \left( E _ n | \left( E _ 1 \cap \cdots \cap E _ { n - 1 } \right) \right) P \left( E _ 1 \cap \cdots \cap E _ { n - 1 } \right) \] whenever the conditional probability is defined.
Let \( A := E _ 1 \cap \cdots \cap E _ { n - 1 } \) and \( B := E _ n \). From intersection as conditional probability, \[ P \left( A \cap B \right) = P \left( B \mid A \right) P \left( A \right). \] Substituting the definitions of \( A \) and \( B \) gives \[ P \left( \left( E _ 1 \cap E _ 2 \cap \cdots \cap E _ { n - 1 } \right) \cap E _ n \right) = P \left( E _ n \mid \left( E _ 1 \cap E _ 2 \cap \cdots \cap E _ { n - 1 } \right) \right) \cdot P \left( E _ 1 \cap \cdots \cap E _ { n - 1 } \right). \]
Product Rule
Let \( E _ 1, \ldots , E _ n \) be events. Repeatedly applying finite intersection as conditional probability gives \[ \begin{aligned} P \left( E _ 1 \cap E _ 2 \cap \cdots \cap E _ n \right) &= P \left( E _ n \mid E _ 1 \cap E _ 2 \cap \cdots \cap E _ { n - 1 } \right) \\ &\quad \cdot P \left( E _ { n - 1 } \mid E _ 1 \cap \cdots \cap E _ { n - 2 } \right) \\ &\quad \cdots P \left( E _ 3 \mid E _ 1 \cap E _ 2 \right) \cdot P \left( E _ 2 \mid E _ 1 \right) P \left( E _ 1 \right). \end{aligned} \]
Proof by induction and using the previous lemma during the induction step and the "intersection as conditional probability" for the base case.

As stated it is notationally unwieldy. We can clean it up using the following: \[ P \left( E _ 1 \cap E _ 2 \cap \cdots \cap E _ n \right) = P \left( E _ 1 \right) \left( \prod _ {j = 2} ^ n P \left( E _ j \mid E _ 1 \cap \cdots \cap E _ { j - 1 } \right)\right) \]

Symmetric Conditional Equation
If \( P \left( A \right) \) and \( P \left( B \right) \) are nonzero, then \[ P \left( A \mid B \right) P \left( B \right) = P \left( B \mid A \right) P \left( A \right) \]
By conditional probability, \[ P \left( A \mid B \right) P \left( B \right) = P \left( A \cap B \right). \] Since \( A \cap B = B \cap A \), we have \[ P \left( A \cap B \right) = P \left( B \cap A \right) = P \left( B \mid A \right) P \left( A \right). \] Therefore \( P \left( A \mid B \right) P \left( B \right) = P \left( B \mid A \right) P \left( A \right) \).
Bayes Formula
If the relevant conditional probabilities are defined, then \[ P \left( A \mid B \right) = \frac{P \left( B \mid A \right) P \left( A \right)}{P \left( B \right)} . \]
By the symmetric conditional equation, \[ P \left( A \mid B \right) P \left( B \right) = P \left( B \mid A \right) P \left( A \right). \] Since \( P \left( B \right) \neq 0 \), divide both sides by \( P \left( B \right) \) to get \[ P \left( A \mid B \right) = \frac{P \left( B \mid A \right) P \left( A \right)}{P \left( B \right)}. \]
Probability of Set Difference
Suppose that \( A, B \subseteq \Omega \) are events. Then \[ P \left( A \setminus B \right) = P \left( A \right) - P \left( A \cap B \right) \]
By set difference decomposition, the event \( A \) decomposes as the disjoint union \[ A = \left( A \setminus B \right) \cup \left( A \cap B \right). \] Since \( A \setminus B \) and \( A \cap B \) are disjoint, the probability measure gives \[ P \left( A \right) = P \left( A \setminus B \right) + P \left( A \cap B \right). \] Subtracting \( P \left( A \cap B \right) \) from both sides gives \[ P \left( A \setminus B \right) = P \left( A \right) - P \left( A \cap B \right). \]
Probability Outside an Event
Suppose that \( A \subseteq \Omega \) is an event. Then \[ P \left( \Omega \setminus A \right) = 1 - P \left( A \right). \]
By probability of set difference, \[ P \left( \Omega \setminus A \right) = P \left( \Omega \right) - P \left( \Omega \cap A \right). \] Since \( A \subseteq \Omega \), we have \( \Omega \cap A = A \). Also, \( P \left( \Omega \right) = 1 \) by the definition of a probability measure. Therefore \[ P \left( \Omega \setminus A \right) = 1 - P \left( A \right). \]
Probability of the Impossible Event
The impossible event has probability zero: \[ P \left( \emptyset \right) = 0. \]
Since \( \Omega \setminus \Omega = \emptyset \), probability outside an event gives \[ P \left( \emptyset \right) = P \left( \Omega \setminus \Omega \right) = 1 - P \left( \Omega \right) = 0. \]
Probability is Nonnegative
If \( A \) is an event, then \[ P \left( A \right) \ge 0. \]
By the definition of a probability measure, \( P \) has codomain \( \left[ 0,1 \right] \), so \( P \left( A \right) \in \left[ 0,1 \right] \).
Monotonicity of Probability
Suppose that \( A \subseteq B \subseteq \Omega \) are events. Then \[ P \left( A \right) \le P \left( B \right). \]
By set difference decomposition, \( B = \left( B \setminus A \right) \cup \left( B \cap A \right) \), and since \( A \subseteq B \), we have \( B \cap A = A \). Thus \( B \) is the disjoint union of \( B \setminus A \) and \( A \). Therefore \[ P \left( B \right) = P \left( B \setminus A \right) + P \left( A \right) \ge P \left( A \right) \] because \( P \left( B \setminus A \right) \ge 0 \) by probability is nonnegative. Thus \[ P \left( B \right) \ge P \left( A \right). \]
Continuity of Probability
Let \( P \) be a probability measure. If \( A _ n \nearrow A \), then \[ \lim _ { n \to \infty } P \left( A _ n \right) = P \left( A \right). \] If \( A _ n \searrow A \), then \[ \lim _ { n \to \infty } P \left( A _ n \right) = P \left( A \right). \]
Suppose first that \( A _ n \nearrow A \). Define \( B _ 1 := A _ 1 \) and \( B _ n := A _ n \setminus A _ { n - 1 } \) for \( n \ge 2 \). The events \( B _ 1,B _ 2,\ldots \) are pairwise disjoint, \( A = \bigcup _ { n=1 } ^ \infty B _ n \), and \( A _ N = \bigcup _ { n=1 } ^ N B _ n \). By countable additivity, \[ P \left( A \right) = \sum _ { n=1 } ^ \infty P \left( B _ n \right), \] while \( P \left( A _ N \right) = \sum _ { n=1 } ^ N P \left( B _ n \right) \). Hence \[ \lim _ { N \to \infty } P \left( A _ N \right) = P \left( A \right). \]

Now suppose that \( A _ n \searrow A \). Then \( A _ 1 \setminus A _ n \nearrow A _ 1 \setminus A \). By the increasing case and probability of set difference,

\[ \begin{aligned} \lim _ { n \to \infty } P \left( A _ n \right) &= \lim _ { n \to \infty } \left( P \left( A _ 1 \right) - P \left( A _ 1 \setminus A _ n \right) \right) \\ &= P \left( A _ 1 \right) - P \left( A _ 1 \setminus A \right) \\ &= P \left( A \right). \end{aligned} \]
Probability of an Event through a Partition
Suppose that \( \mathcal{ X } = \left\{ X _ 1, X _ 2, \ldots \right\} \) is a partition of the sample space \( \Omega \). Then for any event \( E \) we have \[ P \left( E \right) = \sum _ { i \in \mathbb{ N } _ 1 } P \left( E \cap X _ i \right) \]

Observe that \( E = E \cap \Omega = E \cap \left( \sqcup _ { i \in \mathbb{ N } _ 1 } X _ i \right) = \sqcup _ { i \in \mathbb{ N } _ 1 } \left( E \cap X _ i \right) \), therefore \[ P \left( E \right) = \sum _ { i \in \mathbb{ N } _ 1 } P \left( E \cap X _ i \right) \]

It follows as an easy corollary that it holds for finite partitions by setting the rest of the partition to be the empty set.

Law of Total Probability
Suppose that \( \mathcal{ X } = \left\{ X _ 1, X _ 2, \ldots \right\} \) is a partition of \( \Omega \). Combining probability through a partition with conditional probability gives \[ P \left( E \right) = \sum _ { i \in \mathbb{ N } _ 1 } P \left( E \mid X _ i \right) P \left( X _ i \right) \]
Independence of Events
Given two events \( A, B \subseteq \Omega \), we say that \( A \) and \( B \) are independent iff \[ P \left( A \cap B \right) = P \left( A \right)P \left( B \right). \]
Independence is Symmetric
Suppose that \( A \) and \( B \) are independent. Then \( B \) and \( A \) are independent.
Since \( A \cap B = B \cap A \), \[ P \left( B \cap A \right) = P \left( A \cap B \right) = P \left( A \right)P \left( B \right) = P \left( B \right)P \left( A \right). \]
Independent Family of Events
A family of events \( \left\{ A _ i : i \in I \right\} \) is independent if, for every finite \( J \subseteq I \), \[ P \left( \bigcap _ { j \in J } A _ j \right) = \prod _ { j \in J } P \left( A _ j \right). \]
Pairwise Independent Events
A family of events \( \left\{ A _ i : i \in I \right\} \) is pairwise independent if each pair is independent, so \( P \left( A _ i \cap A _ j \right) = P \left( A _ i \right)P \left( A _ j \right) \) whenever \( i \neq j \). Pairwise independence does not, by itself, imply independence of the whole family.
Independence is Preserved by Sample-Space Difference
If \( A \) and \( B \) are independent events, then \( \Omega \setminus A \) and \( B \), \( A \) and \( \Omega \setminus B \), and \( \Omega \setminus A \) and \( \Omega \setminus B \) are independent.
First note that we will use probability outside an event. Now, \[ \begin{align} P \left( \left( \Omega \setminus A \right) \cap B \right) &= P \left( B \setminus A \right) \tag{\text{ambient difference}} \\ &= P \left( B \right) - P \left( A \cap B \right) \tag{\text{probability of set difference}} \\ &= P \left( B \right) - P \left( A \right)P \left( B \right) \tag{\text{independence}} \\ &= \left( 1 - P \left( A \right) \right)P \left( B \right) \tag{\text{algebra}} \\ &= P \left( \Omega \setminus A \right)P \left( B \right). \tag{\text{probability outside event}} \end{align} \] Therefore \( \Omega \setminus A \) and \( B \) are independent. Similarly, \[ \begin{align} P \left( A \cap \left( \Omega \setminus B \right) \right) &= P \left( A \setminus B \right) \tag{\text{ambient difference}} \\ &= P \left( A \right) - P \left( A \cap B \right) \\ &= P \left( A \right) - P \left( A \right)P \left( B \right) \\ &= P \left( A \right)\left( 1 - P \left( B \right) \right) \\ &= P \left( A \right)P \left( \Omega \setminus B \right). \end{align} \] Therefore \( A \) and \( \Omega \setminus B \) are independent. Finally, \[ \begin{align} P \left( \left( \Omega \setminus A \right) \cap \left( \Omega \setminus B \right) \right) &= P \left( \left( \Omega \setminus A \right) \setminus B \right) \tag{\text{intersection of differences}} \\ &= P \left( \Omega \setminus A \right) - P \left( \left( \Omega \setminus A \right) \cap B \right) \\ &= P \left( \Omega \setminus A \right) - P \left( \Omega \setminus A \right)P \left( B \right) \\ &= P \left( \Omega \setminus A \right)\left( 1 - P \left( B \right) \right) \\ &= P \left( \Omega \setminus A \right)P \left( \Omega \setminus B \right). \end{align} \] Therefore \( \Omega \setminus A \) and \( \Omega \setminus B \) are independent.
Product Probability Space
If \( \left( \Omega _ 1,\mathcal{ F } _ 1,P _ 1 \right) \) and \( \left( \Omega _ 2,\mathcal{ F } _ 2,P _ 2 \right) \) are probability spaces, their independent product has sample space \( \Omega _ 1 \times \Omega _ 2 \), events generated by rectangles \( A _ 1 \times A _ 2 \), and probability determined on rectangles by \[ P \left( A _ 1 \times A _ 2 \right) = P _ 1 \left( A _ 1 \right)P _ 2 \left( A _ 2 \right). \]
